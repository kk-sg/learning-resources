## 📖 TALK OVERVIEW

### Why This Tendency Pair Matters

Commitment/Consistency and Stress-Influence are the mechanisms that turn small errors into disasters. Commitment makes us double down on bad decisions because "I've already invested so much" and "I need to be consistent with my past choices." Stress makes us react quickly and emotionally rather than thoughtfully, bypassing our analytical brain. Together, they create the classic "throw good money after bad" pattern.

### Core Idea in One Sentence

We are wired to be consistent with our past choices and to react quickly under stress—both of which serve survival on the savannah but destroy decision-making in complex modern systems.

### Main Problem This Talk Helps You Avoid

Doubling down on bad decisions because "I've already invested so much" and making panic moves under time pressure that you later regret.

### One Memorable Analogy / Phrase (Paraphrased)

"Doing a favor for someone you dislike makes you like them more—to avoid the inconsistency of helping an enemy." This is the Ben Franklin effect: commitment drives us to align our emotions with our actions, even when it's irrational.

## 🎯 CORE FRAMEWORK(S) FROM THIS TALK

### Framework 1: Inconsistency-Avoidance Tendency (#5)

- **What it is:** The brain conserves programming space by being reluctant to change. We want to be consistent with our past commitments and self-image. Changing our mind feels like admitting we were wrong, which our brain resists.
- **How Munger uses it:** This explains why people hold losing stocks ("I said this was a good investment"), stay in bad jobs ("I've been here 10 years"), and defend bad ideas ("I've always believed this"). It also explains positive outcomes like building habits through small commitments.
- **Real-life application:** Be willing to destroy your own best-loved ideas when new evidence appears. "Invert, always invert." Pre-commit to criteria for when you'll change your mind, before you make the initial decision.

**Key Munger Quote:**
> "The Ben Franklin effect: doing a favor for someone you dislike makes you like them more—to avoid the inconsistency of helping an enemy."

### Framework 2: Stress-Influence Tendency (#17)

- **What it is:** Sudden stress (adrenaline) causes faster, more extreme reactions. Less thinking, more habit. We default to what's familiar rather than what's rational.
- **How Munger uses it:** This explains why people panic sell in market crashes, why CEOs make terrible decisions in crises, why people say things they later regret in arguments. The analytical brain shuts down, leaving only emotional reactions.
- **Real-life application:** Pre-commitment. Have rules for stressful times. "I never sell on a red day." "I never make decisions after 10 PM." "I never send angry emails immediately."

**Key Munger Quote:**
> "Under stress, people don't think—they react. The habits they've built determine the reaction. That's why building good habits matters."

### Framework 3: The "Double Down" Death Spiral

- **What it is:** When Commitment/Consistency combines with Deprival-Superreaction (loss aversion) and Denial, we double down on bad decisions. "I can't sell now, I've already invested so much." "If I just buy more, I'll average down and it will come back." This is the pattern that turns a 20% loss into an 80% loss.
- **How Munger uses it:** This explains the dot-com bubble, real estate crashes, corporate disasters where executives keep funding failing projects rather than admit they were wrong.
- **Real-life application:** The antidote is pre-committed exit criteria. "If thesis broken, exit. No exceptions." This removes the emotional decision point from the death spiral.

## 🧠 5 ACTIONABLE TAKEAWAYS FOR DECISION-MAKING

1. **[Behavior Change]** Write down "thesis broken" criteria BEFORE entering any investment, project, or commitment. "If X happens (specific trigger), the thesis is broken and I exit." Example for investing: "If the company misses earnings twice, thesis is broken. I sell." This removes the emotional decision point.

2. **[Checklist Item]** Before doubling down on any position or project, run the "Commitment Check": "Would I enter this fresh today, with no prior investment?" If the answer is "no," you're doubling down due to Inconsistency-Avoidance, not rational analysis. Exit, don't double down.

3. **[Inversion]** Write a "Prescription for Doubling Down Disaster" for your biggest current position. How would you guarantee catastrophic loss using Commitment/Consistency? Example: "Keep investing more to 'prove' you were right. Ignore all data that contradicts you. Tell everyone you're 'committed to the long term' so you can't back out without looking foolish." Then make sure you're not doing those things.

4. **[System Design]** Implement a "No Big Decisions Under Stress" rule. Pre-commit: "I never make decisions over $1,000 / with 6+ month impact when I'm tired, hungry, angry, or rushed. If I feel pressure to decide now, I wait 24 hours minimum." This bypasses Stress-Influence.

5. **[Bias Countermeasure]** When you feel the urge to "prove" a past decision was right, ask: "Am I protecting the outcome, or am I protecting my ego?" Commitment bias makes us feel like changing our mind = admitting defeat. But changing when new evidence appears = smart adaptation. Rename "I was wrong" to "I updated."

## 🧪 5 CASE SNAPSHOTS

### Case Snapshot 1: The Ben Franklin Effect (Commitment/Consistency)

- **Context:** Benjamin Franklin wanted to win over a rival legislator who hated him. Instead of returning the hostility, Franklin asked to borrow a rare book from him. The rival obliged. Later, the rival became Franklin's friend.
- **Mistake or Success Pattern:** Franklin understood Inconsistency-Avoidance. Doing a favor for someone you dislike creates cognitive dissonance ("I hate him, but I helped him"). To resolve the dissonance, the brain adjusts: "I must not hate him that much. Maybe I like him."
- **Relevant Talk Idea:** Inconsistency-Avoidance Tendency (#5)—doing a favor for an enemy makes you like them more, to avoid the inconsistency of helping someone you dislike.
- **Micro-Lesson:** You can hack commitment bias for good. Ask someone to do a small favor for you, and they'll like you more. This is why asking for help builds relationships.

### Case Snapshot 2: The Sunk Cost Trap (Commitment + Deprival + Denial)

- **Context:** A company invests $10M in a new product. It's not working. Instead of canceling, they invest another $10M to "make it work." It's still not working. They invest another $10M. Total loss: $30M.
- **Mistake or Success Pattern:** Classic sunk cost fallacy, driven by: Commitment ("We've already invested so much"), Deprival-Superreaction ("Stopping feels like admitting total loss"), Denial ("It will work if we just try harder"). The rational decision would have been to cancel after the first $10M.
- **Relevant Talk Idea:** When Commitment, Deprival-Superreaction, and Denial combine, they create a death spiral. Smart people throw good money after bad because they can't face the pain of admitting they were wrong.
- **Micro-Lesson:** Sunk costs are sunk. The only relevant question is: "Is this a good use of future resources?" If the answer is "no," exit regardless of what you've already invested.

### Case Snapshot 3: Panic Selling in Market Crashes (Stress-Influence)

- **Context:** March 2020, COVID-19 panic. Markets crashed 30%+ in weeks. Many investors panic-sold at the bottom, locking in losses, then missed the recovery.
- **Mistake or Success Pattern:** Stress-Influence in action. Under extreme stress (falling markets, scary headlines), the analytical brain shuts down. People default to habit: "selling feels safe, holding feels risky." They react emotionally rather than analytically.
- **Relevant Talk Idea:** Stress-Influence Tendency (#17)—under stress, we don't think, we react. We do what's familiar, not what's rational. This is why pre-committed rules ("I never sell on a red day") are essential.
- **Micro-Lesson:** The best time to make rules about crisis behavior is BEFORE the crisis. When panic hits, you won't think clearly. Your pre-committed rules will save you.

### Case Snapshot 4: The Vietnam War Escalation (Commitment + Denial)

- **Context:** U.S. involvement in Vietnam began with military advisors. When that didn't work, more troops were sent. When that didn't work, bombing campaigns began. When that didn't work, more troops were sent. The pattern continued for years.
- **Mistake or Success Pattern:** Classic escalation of commitment. Each new commitment (more troops, more bombing) made it harder to admit the previous commitments were wrong. Denial of reality ("we're winning") persisted despite evidence to the contrary.
- **Relevant Talk Idea:** Inconsistency-Avoidance + Simple Denial creates a trap where each new commitment makes it harder to exit. This pattern appears in wars, corporate projects, and personal decisions.
- **Micro-Lesson:** The most dangerous commitments are those that require admitting previous commitments were wrong. That's why we need pre-committed exit criteria before we start.

### Case Snapshot 5: The "Lock-In" Strategy in Sales (Hacking Commitment)

- **Context:** Car dealerships, real estate agents, and software companies use "lock-in" strategies. Get the customer to make a small commitment (test drive, free trial, small deposit). Then escalate to larger commitments.
- **Mistake or Success Pattern:** They're hacking Inconsistency-Avoidance. Once you've made a small commitment, your brain wants to stay consistent with it. "I took the test drive, so I must be interested. I should buy the car." "I signed up for the trial, so I must like it. I should subscribe."
- **Relevant Talk Idea:** Commitment/Consistency Tendency (#5)—small commitments lead to larger commitments. This is why salespeople start with "yes" questions before asking for the sale.
- **Micro-Lesson:** When someone asks you for a small commitment, ask: "What larger commitment does this lead to?" If you're not willing to make the larger commitment, don't make the small one.

## 📊 DAILY MENTAL MODEL DRILL

- **Today's Primary Models:** Inconsistency-Avoidance Tendency (#5) + Stress-Influence Tendency (#17)

### Day 9 Prompts:

- **"What commitment am I holding onto because exiting would feel like admitting failure?"** Identify one investment, project, or relationship you're locked into. Ask: "Would I start this fresh today?" If "no," you're trapped by Commitment bias. What's your exit criterion?

- **"What would happen if I made this decision under extreme stress?"** Identify a high-stakes decision you might face soon. Write down what you WOULD do under stress (panic sell, angry email, rash purchase) vs. what you SHOULD do (wait, analyze, consult others). Pre-commit to the rational path.

- **"What 'proof' am I seeking to show I was right?"** Commitment bias makes us seek evidence that confirms our past decisions. What are you trying to "prove" rather than "discover"? Shifting from "proving" to "discovering" breaks the consistency trap.

## 5 REFLECTION QUESTIONS

1. **[Self-Audit]** Think of a time you doubled down on a bad decision—investing more time/money, defending a bad idea, staying in a bad situation. What Commitment bias ("I've already invested so much") and what Stress reaction (pressure to act now) were at play? What would have happened if you had exited earlier?

2. **[Investing/Career]** What investments or career commitments are you making based on "I need to be consistent with my past choices" rather than "Is this still a good decision going forward?" This is the Commitment trap. Write down your "thesis broken" criteria.

3. **[Relationships]** What commitments are you keeping out of "consistency" rather than because they're right? A friendship that's toxic but "we've been friends for years"? A job that's wrong for you but "I've invested 10 years"? How would your life change if you exited?

4. **[Business/Work]** What projects is your organization funding because "we've already invested so much" rather than "this will create future value"? This is the corporate sunk cost trap. What would happen if you killed those projects today?

5. **[Latticework Building]** How does Commitment/Consistency relate to "Sunk Cost Fallacy" (economics)? How does Stress-Influence relate to "Fight or Flight" (biology)? These tendencies are connected across disciplines—seeing the connections deepens your understanding.
