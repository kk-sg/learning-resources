# Master Notes: Talk 2 - A Lesson on Elementary, Worldly Wisdom

**Talk Title:** A Lesson on Elementary, Worldly Wisdom as It Relates to Investment Management and Business  
**Speaker:** Charlie Munger  
**Date:** 1994  
**Venue:** USC Business School  
**Duration:** 2 weeks (14 days) in reading challenge  
**Difficulty:** ⭐ 2 WEEKS (Foundational - one of Munger's most important talks)

---

## Executive Summary

This is one of Munger's most famous and influential talks, introducing his core framework for thinking and decision-making. He argues that success in business and investing requires a "latticework of mental models" drawn from multiple disciplines—not just finance. The talk is structured as "carrots before dessert": Munger first explains worldly wisdom (general mental models), then applies these frameworks to stock picking. Key themes include the danger of single-model thinking ("man with a hammer syndrome"), the importance of multidisciplinary knowledge, and the psychology of misjudgment. Munger emphasizes that 80-90 models carry 90% of the freight, with only a handful doing the heavy lifting.

---

## Key Mental Models

### 1. Latticework of Mental Models (Core Framework)

**The Concept:**
You cannot know anything if you just remember isolated facts. Facts must hang together on a latticework of theory to be usable. You must array both direct and vicarious experience on this latticework of models in your head.

**Why Multiple Models Matter:**
- If you only have one or two models, human psychology causes you to torture reality to fit your models
- Single-model thinkers become like chiropractors who see every problem through one lens
- "To the man with only a hammer, every problem looks like a nail"

**The Numbers:**
- 80-90 important models carry about 90% of the freight in making you worldly-wise
- Of those, only a mere handful carry very heavy freight
- Models must come from multiple disciplines—all wisdom isn't in one academic department

**The Structure:**
```
Hard Sciences & Engineering (Most Reliable)
    ↓
Biology/Physiology
    ↓
Psychology
    ↓
Microeconomics
```

**Why Poetry Professors Are Unwise:**
Munger jokes that poetry professors are unwise in worldly terms because they don't have enough models in their heads—they're trained in one narrow discipline.

---

### 2. Man with a Hammer Syndrome

**The Problem:**
People trained in one field tend to view every problem through that single lens. This is disastrous for thinking and operating in the world.

**Examples Munger Gives:**
- Chiropractors who see all health problems as spinal issues
- Economists who reduce everything to supply and demand
- Academics who use their discipline's framework for everything

**The Solution:**
Build a diverse toolkit of mental models from multiple disciplines so you can approach problems from different angles.

**Related Concept:**
This connects to what Munger calls "bias from commitment and consistency"—when you've invested in one way of thinking, it's hard to change course.

---

### 3. Elementary Mathematics (Fermat/Pascal System)

**The Foundation:**
After compound interest, the most useful mathematical model is elementary permutations and combinations (basic probability).

**Key Points:**
- Developed by Pascal and Fermat through letters over about one year
- Simple algebra, not advanced mathematics
- Harvard Business School calls this "decision tree theory"—applying high school algebra to real-life problems

**Why People Struggle:**
- The brain's neural network evolved for crude, shortcut approximations
- It's NOT naturally wired for Fermat/Pascal thinking
- You must learn to use it "unnaturally," like learning a proper golf swing

**Munger's Warning:**
"If you don't get this elementary, but mildly unnatural, mathematics of elementary probability into your repertoire, then you go through a long life like a one-legged man in an ass-kicking contest."

**Buffett's Advantage:**
Buffett automatically thinks in terms of decision trees and elementary probability—it's part of what makes him exceptional.

---

### 4. Accounting as Language (and Its Limitations)

**Importance:**
Accounting is the language of practical business life—a great gift to civilization (originated in Venice).

**The Critical Caveat:**
You must know enough accounting to understand its LIMITATIONS. It's only a crude approximation of reality.

**Example - Depreciation:**
Just because you express depreciation in neat numbers doesn't mean you actually know the useful life of a jet airplane. It's a guess dressed up in precision.

**Carl Braun Story:**
Braun (CF Braun Engineering Company) threw out standard accountants and had engineers create their own accounting system for building oil refineries. Standard accounting was "asinine" for their purposes. Later, the accounting profession adopted many of Braun's innovations.

**The Five W's Rule (Braun's Psychological Insight):**
- Every communication had to answer: Who, What, Where, When, and WHY
- If you didn't include the "why," you could get fired
- People understand better, consider it more important, and are more likely to comply when they know the reason
- Even if they don't understand your reason, they're more likely to comply

**Application:**
Just as you build worldly wisdom by asking "why, why, why," you should always include "why" when communicating with others.

---

### 5. The Psychology of Misjudgment

**Munger's Assessment:**
Psychology is "ungodly important" for worldly wisdom. The useful parts can be taught to any intelligent person in a week, but Munger had to learn it piecemeal over a lifetime because elite institutions didn't teach it.

**Key Insight - The Magician Principle:**
A professional magician demonstrates that:
- Everyone in the room sees things that aren't happening
- Everyone fails to see things that are happening
- The brain has shortcuts in its perceptual apparatus (limited circuitry)
- Those who understand these shortcuts can manipulate what you perceive

**Cognitive vs. Perceptual:**
- Perceptual: Your brain takes shortcuts in processing visual information
- Cognitive: Your brain takes shortcuts in thinking and judgment
- Both make you vulnerable to manipulation—by circumstances or by other people

**Pascal's Observation:**
"The mind of man at one and the same time is both the glory and the shame of the universe."
- Enormous power
- Standard misfunctions that cause wrong conclusions
- Extraordinarily subject to manipulation by others

**Example - Half of Hitler's Army:**
Roughly half of Hitler's army was composed of believing Catholics. This demonstrates what clever psychological manipulation can achieve—good people doing terrible things.

**Munger's Two-Track Analysis:**
1. **Rational Track:** What are the factors that really govern the interests involved? (Like working out a bridge problem—evaluate real interests and probabilities)
2. **Psychological Track:** What are the subconscious influences where the brain is automatically doing things that usually help but often misfunction?

**The 20 Principles:**
Munger references "about 20 little principles" of psychology that interact and get complicated, but the core is unbelievably important. Even terribly smart people make bonkers mistakes by ignoring these.

**Munger's Humility:**
"I've done it several times during the last two or three years in a very important way. You never get totally over making silly mistakes."

---

### 6. Microeconomics as Ecosystem

**The Unfashionable Truth:**
Munger finds it useful to think of a free-market economy as an ecosystem. This became unfashionable because robber barons used "survival of the fittest" to justify their power, but the analogy is accurate.

**Niche Specialization:**
Just as animals flourish in ecological niches, people who specialize narrowly in business can find good economics they wouldn't get otherwise.

---

### 7. Advantages of Scale (Extensive Coverage)

Munger devotes significant time to advantages of scale—critical for understanding which businesses succeed or fail.

#### Types of Scale Advantages:

**A. Geometric Advantages:**
- Building a spherical tank: surface area increases by square, volume by cube
- Larger dimensions mean more volume per unit of material
- Simple reality gives natural scale advantages

**B. TV Advertising (Historical Example):**
- When TV arrived, three networks had 90% of the audience
- Procter & Gamble could afford expensive network TV because they sold huge volume
- Small competitors couldn't buy partial access
- Big branded companies got a huge tailwind
- They prospered until some got "fat and foolish"

**C. Informational Advantage:**
- Wrigley's gum vs. unknown brand in remote location
- Even if unknown brand is cheaper, people won't risk putting something unfamiliar in their mouth for a small saving
- Being well-known creates trust and reduces customer risk

**D. Social Proof (Psychological Advantage):**
- We're influenced by what others do and approve
- If everyone's buying something, we think it's better
- We don't like being the one person out of step
- Coca-Cola's worldwide distribution = huge advantage
- Once you have enough of these advantages, it becomes very hard to be dislodged

**E. "Cascade to Winner-Take-All" (Newspapers):**
- Daily newspapers: almost no US city has more than one
- Once you get most circulation, you get most advertising
- Once you have most advertising and circulation, why would anyone want the thinner paper?
- Cascades to winner-take-all situation
- Separate form of scale advantage

**F. Greater Specialization:**
- Huge scale allows specialization within the firm
- Each person can be better at what they do
- Jack Welch's GE: "Be #1 or #2 in every field or get out"
- Ruthless but correct for maximizing shareholder wealth

---

### 8. Disadvantages of Scale (Bureaucracy)

**The Constant Curse:**
As you get big, you get bureaucracy—and bureaucracy is terrible.

**Characteristics of Bureaucracy:**
- Territoriality (grounded in human nature)
- Perverse incentives
- Work is considered "done" when it leaves your in-basket (not when it's actually delivered)
- Big, fat, dumb, unmotivated organizations

**The "Won't Bother Me" Corruption:**
- If I have a department and you have a department
- Unwritten rule: "If you won't bother me, I won't bother you, and we're both happy"
- Layers of management and costs nobody needs
- Takes forever to get anything done
- Nimbler people run circles around them

**Westinghouse Example:**
- Blowed billions on dumb loans to real estate developers
- Put someone from a career path (maybe refrigerators) in charge of lending to real estate developers building hotels
- Very unequal contest—lost billions

**CBS/Paley Example - Pavlovian Association:**
- Bill Paley didn't like to hear unpleasant things
- People learned to tell him only what he liked to hear
- He lived in a "cocoon of unreality"
- CBS acquisitions after the network purchase were absolutely terrible
- Gave 20% of CBS to Dumont Company for a TV manufacturer that lasted 2-3 years
- Dysfunction in big, powerful places where no one brings unwelcome reality to the boss

**Government as Worst Case:**
Bureaucracy reaches its "highest and worst form" in government where incentives are really awful—but we still need government.

**Fighting Bureaucracy:**
- Decentralized units
- Fancy motivation and training programs
- Jack Welch at GE: genius + fanatic + put in young enough for a long run

---

### 9. Chain Store Model

**Why Chain Stores Work:**
1. **Huge purchasing power** = lower merchandise costs
2. **Many "laboratories"** for experiments across stores
3. **Specialization** in buying (experts for each category vs. one person buying 27 categories)
4. **Systems** that force everyone to do what works

**The Salt Story:**
Little store with salt all over walls. Stranger: "You must sell a lot of salt." Owner: "No, I don't. But you should see the guy who sells me salt."
(Shows how unsophisticated small buyers get taken advantage of by suppliers)

---

### 10. Wal-Mart vs. Sears Case Study

**How Wal-Mart Beat Sears:**

Sam Walton started from ONE store in Bentonville, Arkansas against Sears with all its billions and reputation—and won in his own lifetime.

**Walton's Strategy:**
1. Invented practically nothing—copied everything smart
2. Did it with more fanaticism and better employee manipulation
3. Early strategy: "Fought 42 palookas" (knocked out small town merchants one by one)
4. Used better system to destroy small operators first
5. Then started destroying the big boys

**Munger's Assessment:**
"Is this a nice way to behave? Capitalism is a pretty brutal place. But I personally think the world is better for having Wal-Mart."

Don't idealize small-town life or the businesses he destroyed. Many Wal-Mart employees are "high grade, bouncy people raising nice children."

**Sears' Failure:**
- Layers of people it didn't need
- Bureaucratic and slow to think
- Established way of thinking—new ideas got crushed by the system
- Everything dysfunctional you'd expect from a big bureaucracy
- All its scale advantages weren't enough

---

### 11. The Cereal vs. Airlines Paradox

**The Puzzle:**
Some markets with 2-3 big competitors: everyone does well (cereals). Others: no one makes money (airlines).

**Airlines:**
- Given the world safe travel, time with loved ones, incredible value
- Net money made by airline shareholders since Kitty Hawk: NEGATIVE (substantial negative)
- Competition so intense after deregulation that it ravaged shareholder wealth

**Cereals:**
- Medium-grade cereal maker: maybe 15% return on capital
- Really good: maybe 40%
- Despite heavy promotion, coupons, apparent competition

**Munger's Hypothesis:**
Brand identity factor in cereals that doesn't exist in airlines. Also, cereal makers may have learned not to fight too hard for market share.

**The Danger:**
"If I were Kellogg and decided I had to have 60% of the market, I think I could take most of the profit out of cereals. I'd ruin Kellogg in the process. But I think I could do it."

**The Uncertainty:**
Munger admits he doesn't have a perfect model for predicting when competition will be rational vs. destructive.

**Coke/Pepsi Bottlers:**
Some markets both make lots of money, others they destroy most profitability. Comes down to "peculiarities of individual adjustment to market capitalism"—you'd have to know the people involved.

---

### 12. Patents, Trademarks, and Franchises

**Patents:**
- When Munger was young: more money went into patents than came out
- Judges threw them out based on prior art arguments
- Then changed administration to one pro-patent court
- Now people starting to make money from patents

**Trademarks:**
Always made people a lot of money. Wonderful for big, well-known operations.

**Exclusive Franchises:**
- Only three TV channels in a city (pre-cable): natural oligopoly position
- Only food stand in an airport: captive clientele, small monopoly

---

### 13. Technology: When It Helps vs. Kills You

**The Great Lesson:**
Discriminate between when technology will help you and when it will kill you. Most people don't get this straight. Buffett does.

**Berkshire Textile Business Example:**
- Terrible commodity business, low-end textiles
- Workers came to Warren: "New loom does twice as much work!"
- Warren: "Gee, I hope this doesn't work because if it does, I'm going to close the mill"

**Warren's Logic:**
- Lousy business earning substandard returns
- Kept open only to be nice to elderly workers
- Productivity increases in commodity business → all benefits flow to CUSTOMERS
- Nothing sticks to the owners' ribs

**The General Principle:**
Wonderful inventions in commodity businesses give owners nothing except opportunity to spend more money in a still-lousy business. All advantages flow through to customers.

**Newspaper Counter-Example:**
Own the only newspaper in Oshkosh → more efficient production → all savings go to YOUR bottom line.

**The Projections Fallacy:**
Every projection shows: "This capital outlay will pay for itself in 3 years"
- Based on cost savings at current prices
- Never includes second step: how much stays home vs. flows to customers?
- After 20 years of "3-year payback" investments: earned only 4% per annum

**The Simple Forgotten Idea:**
Machines WERE better. Cost reductions DID happen. But benefits didn't go to the equipment buyer.

---

### 14. Competitive Destruction and the "Surfing" Model

**Competitive Destruction:**
When technology moves fast, you get buggy whip factories destroyed by horseless carriages. Happens again and again.

**The "Surfing" Model:**
- When you're an early bird on a new technology wave, you can "surf" for a long time
- Get up on the wave, stay there, go a long way
- Get off the wave → mired in shallows

**Examples of Successful Surfers:**
- Microsoft
- Intel
- National Cash Register (early days)

**The Cash Register Story (Patterson/NCR):**
1. Patterson was a small retailer losing money
2. Bought a crude cash register → instantly profitable (employees couldn't steal as easily)
3. Instead of thinking "good for my retail business," thought "I'm going into the cash register business"
4. Created National Cash Register
5. Surfed: best distribution, biggest patents, fanatic about everything important
6. "A well-educated orangutan could see that buying into partnership with Patterson was a 100% cinch"

**That's What Investors Should Look For:**
In a long life, you can profit heavily from a few such opportunities—if you develop the wisdom and will to seize them.

**Berkshire's Approach:**
They DON'T invest in high-tech surfers. They're "cranky and idiosyncratic." They feel at a big disadvantage in understanding software, chips, etc. So they avoid it based on personal inadequacies.

---

### 15. Circle of Competence

**The Concept:**
Every person has a circle of competence, and it's very hard to advance that circle.

**Munger's Self-Assessment:**
"If I had to make my living as a musician... I can't even think of a level low enough to describe where I would be sorted out to."

**The Rule:**
- Figure out your own aptitudes
- If you play games where others have aptitudes and you don't, you're going to lose
- That's as close to certain as any prediction you can make
- Figure out where you've got an edge
- Play within your own circle of competence

**World-Class vs. Attainable:**
- Best tennis player in the world: probably hopeless for most
- Best plumbing contractor in Bemidji: doable for two-thirds of people
  - Takes will and intelligence
  - Gradually know all about plumbing in Bemidji
  - Master the art through discipline

**Some Edges Can Be Acquired:**
The game of life for most of us is trying to be something like a good plumbing contractor in Bemidji. Very few are chosen to win world chess tournaments.

**For Others:**
Some may find opportunities surfing in high-tech (Intel, Microsoft). Just because Berkshire avoids it doesn't mean it's irrational for you.

---

### 16. The Stock Market as Pari-Mutuel System

**Efficient Market Theory:**
Munger calls extreme efficient market theory "bonkers"—intellectually consistent, enables pretty mathematics, but fundamental assumption doesn't tie to reality.

**The Better Model—Racetrack Pari-Mutuel:**
- Everyone bets, odds change based on what's bet
- That's what happens in the stock market
- Any fool can see the better horse—but at what odds?

**The Track Take:**
- Track takes 17% off the top
- Must outwit other bettors by huge margin just to break even
- Some people DO beat the system—but rarely

**Munger's Poker Friend:**
Made a substantial living betting only on harness races (less efficient market). Bet only occasionally when seeing mispriced bet. After paying full 17% handle, still made good money.

**Stock Market Advantage:**
House handle is MUCH lower than racetrack. With enough fanaticism and discipline, shrewd people get better than average results.

**The Key:**
The one thing all winning bettors throughout history have in common: **They bet very seldom.**

---

### 17. The Buffett/Munger Approach

**The Wisdom:**
- It's not given to humans to know everything about everything all the time
- But humans who work hard can occasionally find a mispriced bet
- The wise ones bet heavily when the world offers that opportunity
- They bet big when they have the odds
- The rest of the time, they don't
- It's just that simple

**The Reality:**
- In investment management, practically NOBODY operates this way
- Buffett and Munger do
- They're not alone, but a huge majority have "some other crazy construct in their heads"
- Instead of waiting for a near cinch and loading up, most do the opposite

## Practical Applications

### For Personal Development:
1. **Build your latticework systematically**—study multiple disciplines, not just your field
2. **Practice the Five W's**—always explain "why" in communications
3. **Use two-track analysis**—rational evaluation + psychological factors
4. **Identify your circle of competence**—play where you have an edge
5. **Learn elementary probability**—make it automatic, not natural

### For Business Analysis:
1. **Check for scale advantages**: geometry, information, social proof, specialization
2. **Watch for bureaucratic creep**: layers, territoriality, slow decisions
3. **Assess technology impact**: who benefits—owner or customer?
4. **Look for surfing opportunities**: early position in new waves
5. **Evaluate competitive dynamics**: rational or destructive?

### For Investment:
1. **Think like a pari-mutuel bettor**: wait for mispriced bets
2. **Bet seldom**: only when odds are heavily in your favor
3. **Bet big when right**: don't dab around the edges
4. **Stay in your circle**: don't pretend to understand what you don't
5. **Understand market efficiency**: partly efficient, partly not

## Discussion Questions

1. **Model Building:** What are the 3-5 mental models you use most frequently? What disciplines are missing from your latticework?

2. **Hammer Syndrome:** In your profession or field, what's the equivalent of "to a man with a hammer, everything looks like a nail"? How might you counter this?

3. **Circle of Competence:** What is your circle of competence? Where are you trying to operate outside it?

4. **Probability Thinking:** Do you automatically think in terms of decision trees and probability? If not, what would help you develop this skill?

5. **Scale Analysis:** Think of a business you know well. What are its advantages of scale? What bureaucratic disadvantages has it developed?

6. **Technology Assessment:** When you see a new technology, do you automatically ask "who captures the value—the business or its customers?"

7. **Investment Behavior:** How often do you make investment decisions? Is this "betting seldom" or too frequent?

8. **Psychology of Misjudgment:** What cognitive biases have affected your recent important decisions?

9. **Two-Track Analysis:** Try applying Munger's two-track approach to a current decision: What are the rational factors? What psychological factors might be influencing you?

10. **The Five W's:** In your communications this week, are you including "why"? What happens when you do?
