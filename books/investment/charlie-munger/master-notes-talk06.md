# Master Notes: Talk 6 – Investment Practices of Leading Charitable Foundations

## Talk Context

- **When:** October 14, 1998
- **Where:** Miramar Sheraton Hotel, Santa Monica, CA
- **Audience:** Foundation Financial Officers Group sponsored by The Conrad Hilton Foundation, The Amateur Athletic Foundation, The J. Paul Getty Trust, and Rio Hondo Memorial Foundation
- **Why:** Munger was asked by friend John Argue to speak frankly and irreverently about current investment practices in charitable foundations, which were drifting toward excessive complexity and cost

## Core Argument

Charitable foundations are wasting approximately 3% of their net worth annually on complex, high-cost investment practices (multiple layers of consultants, frequent trading, "fund of funds" structures) when they could achieve superior long-term results through simpler approaches—either broad indexing or Berkshire Hathaway-style concentrated, low-turnover buy-and-hold investing.

## Structure Outline

1. **Introduction:** Munger's irreverent invitation to critique current practices
2. **The Drift Toward Complexity:** How foundations are following Yale into "fund of funds"
3. **The Three-Layer Consultant Problem:** Multiple layers creating perverse incentives
4. **The 3% "Croupier's Cost":** The arithmetic problem destroying foundation wealth
5. **Overconfidence in Professionalism:** How excessive professionalism creates overconfidence
6. **Three Options for Foundations:** Indexing, Berkshire-style concentration, or LBO partnerships
7. **The Concentration vs. Diversification Debate:** Why 90% concentration in one equity can be rational
8. **Foreign Investment Considerations:** Direct ownership vs. direct foreign investment
9. **Social Concerns:** Brainpower going into money management rather than value creation
10. **Conclusion:** Imitate Ben Franklin and Berkshire's approach

## Key Examples/Case Studies (from Munger)

1. **Bernie Cornfeld's "Fund of Funds"** - Disgraced model that universities and foundations are now emulating through complex consultant structures

2. **General Motors' Fourth Door Mistake** - GM conducted sophisticated consumer surveys and decided not to put a fourth door in a truck designed to serve as a comfortable five-passenger car. Competitors with more basic methods had actually observed five people entering and exiting cars. This "lollapalooza" of professionalism, surveys, and ignoring human nature cost hundreds of millions.

3. **Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM) Collapse** - Hedge fund with highly leveraged methods collapsed despite principals averaging IQ of 160. Shows that smart, hard-working people are not exempt from professional disasters from overconfidence.

4. **Woodruff Foundations** - Proven extremely wise by retaining approximately 90% concentration in founder's Coca-Cola stock. Munger wonders how much better off all American foundations would be if they had never sold founder stock.

5. **Ben Franklin's Charitable Endowment** - Franklin required concentration in admired companies as part of his charitable bequest, showing this is not a new or radical idea.

6. **General Electric's LBO Competition** - GE can now buy more than $10 billion worth per year with 100% debt financing at rates only slightly higher than U.S. Government, representing "supercompetition" in the LBO field.

7. **Swedish Driver Survey** - 90% of automobile drivers consider themselves above average, illustrating overconfidence bias.

8. **Demosthenes' Wisdom** - "What a man wishes, he will believe" - from five centuries before Christ.

## Inversion Themes (for Day 3)

- **How to guarantee foundation shrinkage:** Maintain 3% annual costs in a low-return environment (5% returns - 3% costs - 5% donations = -3% shrinkage)
- **How to create professional disaster:** Combine overconfidence, sophisticated methods, leverage, and lack of self-criticism (as seen in GM and LTCM)
- **How to waste charitable capital:** Layer upon layer of consultants, each taking fees and creating perverse incentives, ultimately destroying more wealth than is donated
- **How to attract the wrong talent:** Create a profession (money management) that offers high fees for social value transfer rather than wealth creation

## Psychology/Biases (for Day 5)

- **Overconfidence Bias:** Investment experts universally believe they are above average, despite mathematical impossibility (50% must be below median)
- **Incentive-Caused Bias in Consultants:** Consultants profit from activity, turnover, and complexity, not from long-term foundation success
- **Social Proof:** Foundations copy Yale's "endowment model" without questioning if it fits their situation
- **Availability Bias:** Assuming recent high returns (17% real) will continue indefinitely, ignoring historical norms
- **Man-with-a-Hammer Syndrome:** Using complex consultant structures and "fund of funds" because that's what elite institutions do
- **Twaddle Tendency:** Accepting nonsense from "investment experts" who benefit from complexity
- **Liking/Loving Tendency:** Foundations prefer familiar, complex systems that seem professional over simpler, effective approaches
- **Authority-Misinfluence Tendency:** Deferring to multiple layers of "expert" consultants who may have misaligned incentives

## Career Applications (for Day 4)

- **For Investment Professionals:** Recognize that your fees and activities may be destroying client wealth, especially in low-return environments
- **For Foundation Trustees:** Question whether complex consultant structures add value or merely add cost and friction
- **For Corporate Managers:** Learn from GM's mistake—sophisticated methods and surveys cannot replace simple observation of human behavior
- **For Career Planning:** Consider whether working in money management (social value transfer) vs. productive businesses (value creation) aligns with your goals
- **For Decision-Makers:** Apply Feynman's principle of self-criticism—systematically challenge your own assumptions and methods

## External Case Candidates (for Day 6)

### Historical Business Cases
- **Long-Term Capital Management (1998)** - Nobel laureates, 160 IQ principals, sophisticated models, yet collapsed from leverage and overconfidence
- **General Motors Fourth Door Decision** - Professional research vs. simple observation; lost hundreds of millions
- **Bernie Cornfeld's IOS Fund of Funds** - Failed model that institutions now emulate through complex consultant structures

### Contemporary Examples
- **University Endowment Arms Race** - Endowments increasingly hiring hedge funds, private equity, and complex alternatives, often with unclear net benefit
- **Consultant-Led Foundation Portfolios** - Foundations with 50+ positions, multiple managers, and 2-3% annual costs consistently underperforming simple benchmarks
- **The "Yale Model" Proliferation** - Many foundations copying Yale's asset allocation without Yale's scale, access, or expertise

### Personal/Relatable Scenarios
- **The Professional Who Won't Simplify** - Investor who believes more research, more trades, and more analysis must produce better results, ignoring costs
- **The Self-Assessment Trap** - Professional rating themselves above average despite mathematical impossibility (like Swedish drivers)
- **The Copycat Foundation** - Following Yale's lead because "smart people are doing it" without considering different circumstances

### Munger/Berkshire Examples
- **Berkshire's Concentrated Portfolio** - Virtually zero turnover, few stocks, costs below 0.1% annually
- **Coca-Cola Concentration** - Berkshire's large position; Woodruff Foundation's 90% concentration; both have worked well
- **Foreign Investment Through U.S. Companies** - Berkshire owns Coca-Cola, Gillette, etc. rather than direct foreign holdings, getting foreign exposure with familiar companies

### Inversion Cases (What NOT to Do)
- **The Foundation That Layered Consultants** - Three layers: consultants choosing consultants, investment counselors chosen by consultants, analysts influencing counselors—each taking fees
- **The Foundation That Turned Over** - Constantly buying and selling positions based on consultant recommendations, paying commissions, spreads, and taxes
- **The Foundation That Chased Complexity** - Adding LBO funds, hedge funds, private equity, and exotic trades without understanding risks

## Key Data Points

- **Cost Problem:** 3% of foundation net worth per annum wasted on investment management and frictional costs
- **Arithmetic in Good Times:** 17% gross return - 3% costs - 5% donations = 9% net growth
- **Arithmetic in Bad Times:** 5% gross return - 3% costs - 5% donations = -3% annual shrinkage
- **Berkshire Cost:** Below 0.1% of principal per annum (10x lower than foundations)
- **LTCM IQ:** Principals averaged 160
- **Swedish Driver Overconfidence:** 90% rate themselves above average
- **Woodruff Foundation Concentration:** Approximately 90% in Coca-Cola stock

## Important Concepts Defined

- **Fund of Funds:** Investment structure where a fund invests in other funds rather than directly in securities—adds layer of costs and complexity
- **Soft Dollars:** Commissions rebated from brokers to money managers, creating perverse incentives to generate trading
- **Croupiers' Take:** Munger's term for the costs extracted by the investment management industry, like casino house taking
- **Lollapalooza Effect:** Multiple psychological tendencies or forces combining to produce extreme outcomes
- **Beta:** Academic measure of volatility relative to market—used by consultants to justify diversification
- **Supercompetition:** Extreme competition beyond ordinary levels, as seen in LBO market with GE as buyer
- **Carry:** Fee structure in private equity (management fee + performance carry for both general partners and investment managers)

## Munger's Recommended Approach

1. **Simplify:** Eliminate multiple consultant layers
2. **Reduce Turnover:** Hold positions long-term to minimize frictional costs
3. **Concentrate:** Invest in few businesses you deeply understand and admire
4. **Index or Concentrate:** Choose either broad indexing OR concentrated holdings, not complex middle ground
5. **Avoid High-Cost Structures:** LBO funds, hedge funds, and complex alternatives often destroy value
6. **Consider Foreign Exposure Carefully:** Prefer U.S. companies with global operations over direct foreign investment
7. **Ignore Conventional Wisdom:** Just because "everybody diversifies" doesn't make it right for your situation
8. **Apply Self-Criticism:** Systematically challenge your own assumptions (Feynman principle)

## Counterarguments to Consider

- **Liquidity Concerns:** Concentrated positions are less liquid; but foundations have long time horizons
- **Single-Company Risk:** Concentration exposes to company-specific risk; but understanding reduces this risk
- **Fiduciary Duty:** Some argue trustees must diversify; Munger argues excessive diversification IS the breach of fiduciary duty
- **Consultant Expertise:** Some consultants provide value in specialized areas (e.g., emerging markets); but costs often exceed benefits
- **Index Capacity Risk:** If everyone indexes, indexing becomes less effective; but this is not currently a problem
