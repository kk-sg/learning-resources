# Master Notes: Talk 10 – USC Gould School of Law Commencement Address

## Talk Context

- **When:** May 13, 2007
- **Where:** USC Gould School of Law Commencement, Los Angeles, California
- **Audience:** Law school graduates, faculty, family, and friends (approximately 300 graduates)
- **Why:** Commencement address to share life wisdom with entering legal professionals. Munger was 84 years old at the time, offering the accumulated wisdom of a lifetime as a lawyer, investor, and businessman.
- **Occasion:** Keynote speech at law school graduation ceremony

## Core Argument

The safest way to get what you want in life is to deserve what you want by delivering value to others and cultivating deserved trust. This approach, combined with continuous learning, multidisciplinary thinking, and disciplined habits, leads to success and fulfillment. The highest form of civilization is a seamless web of deserved trust where reliable people correctly trust one another with minimal bureaucracy.

## Structure Outline

1. **Opening Principle:** Deserve what you want, deliver what you would buy if on the other end
2. **Admiration-Based Love:** Love should be based on admiration and include instructive figures from the past
3. **Wisdom as Moral Duty:** Lifetime learning is not optional; wisdom acquisition is a moral obligation
4. **Multidisciplinary Approach:** Build a mental latticework of big ideas from all disciplines
5. **Inversion Thinking:** Turn problems around in reverse to solve them more easily
6. **What to Avoid (Failure Modes):** Sloth, unreliability, ideology, self-serving bias, perverse incentives, perverse associations
7. **Objectivity Maintenance:** Darwin's attention to disconfirming evidence, checklist routines
8. **Non-Egality:** Get power into the hands of the most capable (Planck knowledge vs. chauffeur knowledge)
9. **Essential Qualities:** Intense interest in the subject, assiduity (sit down until done)
10. **Handling Life's Blows:** Epictetus's attitude—use every setback as an opportunity to behave well and learn
11. **Anticipating Trouble:** Be ready for adversity; prepare in advance
12. **Conclusion:** The highest form of civilization—a seamless web of deserved trust

## Key Examples/Case Studies (from Munger)

### 1. Warren Buffett as Learning Machine

**Context:** Munger's partner at Berkshire Hathaway
**Insight:** Half of Buffett's time is sitting on his ass and reading; the other half is talking to highly gifted people he trusts and who trust him. He's a continuous learning machine who goes to bed a little wiser each day.
**Lesson:** The skills that got Berkshire through one decade wouldn't suffice for the next. Continuous learning is essential.

### 2. John Wooden's Basketball Coaching

**Context:** Legendary UCLA basketball coach, #1 in world
**Insight:** Wooden told bottom 5 players they don't get to play; top 7 did all the playing. This meant top 7 learned more, and Wooden won more than ever.
**Lesson:** Get practice into hands of people with most aptitude and tendency to be learning machines. Non-egality works better than equal distribution.

### 3. Max Planck and His Chauffeur

**Context:** Planck won Nobel Prize, toured giving speeches. Chauffeur memorized speech and asked to pretend to be Planck in Munich while Planck pretended to be chauffeur.
**Story:** After giving the speech successfully, someone asked a tough question. The chauffeur said: "I can't believe someone in such an advanced city as Munich would ask such an elementary question, so I'm going to ask my chauffeur (Planck) to reply."
**Lesson:** Two kinds of knowledge—Planck knowledge (deep understanding) vs. chauffeur knowledge (memorized talk). The challenge is getting responsibility to Planck-knowledge people, not chauffeur-knowledge people.

### 4. Mozart's Financial Mismanagement

**Context:** Mozart became most famous composer in world but was utterly miserable most of time
**Insight:** One reason was he always overspent his income—self-serving bias in action.
**Lesson:** If Mozart couldn't get away with this asinine conduct, you shouldn't try either. Avoid entitlement thinking about your finances.

### 5. Epictetus and Life's Blows

**Context:** Stoic philosopher, a slave maimed in body, lived in ultimate poverty
**Insight:** Epictetus said every missed chance in life was an opportunity to behave well and learn something. His epitaph: "Here lies Epictetus, a slave maimed in body, the ultimate in poverty, and the favored of the gods."
**Lesson:** Don't be submerged in self-pity. Use terrible blows constructively. Your duty is to behave well and learn from setbacks.

### 6. Scandinavian Canoeists and the Aron Rapids

**Context:** Canoeists who succeeded in taming all rapids of Scandinavia, decided to tackle whirlpools of Aron Rapids in US
**Insight:** Death rate was 100%
**Lesson:** A big whirlpool (like a really deep ideology) is not something you want to go into. Extreme ideology cabbages up your mind—avoid it.

### 7. Judge Munger's Prudence

**Context:** Charlie's grandfather, a federal judge in era before pensions for judges
**Insight:** Under-spent his income all his life, left grandmother in comfortable circumstances. When Charlie's uncle's bank failed in 1930s, Judge Munger took over 1/3 of good assets in exchange for bad assets, bailing out the bank.
**Lesson:** Anticipate trouble, be ready when it comes. Prudence pays off. Charlie remembered this example from college.

### 8. Two-Man Partnership's Assiduity Rule

**Context:** Two partners creating design, build, construction team
**Insight:** Rule: if ever behind on commitments to others, both work 14 hours a day until caught up
**Lesson:** The firm didn't fail; partners died rich. Simple idea—assiduity works.

### 9. Cicero on History

**Context:** Roman philosopher
**Quote:** "The man who doesn't know what happened before he was born goes through life like a child"
**Lesson:** Generalize Cicero—know big ideas from all disciplines, not just history. Build them into a mental latticework.

### 10. Ben Franklin on Persuasion

**Context:** Salomon Brothers case mentioned
**Quote:** "If you would persuade, appeal to interest not to reason"
**Lesson:** When dealing with others' self-serving bias, appeal to their interests rather than trying to reason with them.

## Quotes to Paraphrase

- **"Safest way to get what you want is to deserve what you want"** → Success comes from earning it through merit and delivering value to others, not from taking shortcuts.

- **"Deliver to the world what you would buy if you were on the other end"** → Create products and services that you would gladly purchase if you were the customer. This is how you earn deserved trust.

- **"There is huge pleasure in life to be obtained from getting deserved trust"** → The satisfaction of being trusted by others because you've earned it through reliability is one of life's great pleasures.

- **"Wisdom acquisition is a moral duty"** → Learning throughout life is not optional; it's your obligation. You won't advance based on what you already know—you'll advance by what you learn after you leave here.

- **"I constantly see people rise in life who are not the smartest, sometimes not even the most diligent, but they are learning machines"** → Being a continuous learner is more valuable than raw intelligence or hard work alone.

- **"You go to bed every night a little wiser than when you got up"** → Make incremental learning a daily habit. These small gains compound over a lifetime.

- **"Multidisciplinary approach made me enormously rich"** → Combining knowledge from multiple disciplines in a mental latticework is practical and valuable.

- **"Inversion will help you solve problems unless you're more gifted than Einstein"** → Thinking backwards—asking what will cause failure—is a powerful problem-solving tool accessible to anyone.

- **"If you're unreliable it doesn't matter what your virtues are"** → Reliability is fundamental. Doing what you've committed to do must be automatic.

- **"Extreme ideology cabbages up one's mind"** | Deep ideological commitment prevents clear thinking. Avoid becoming an unthinking follower.

- **"I'm not entitled to have an opinion on this subject unless I can state the arguments against my position better than the people who support it"** → Only when you can articulate the best opposing arguments are you qualified to have an opinion. This prevents ideological lock-in.

- **"I think I've just described practically every politician in the United States"** → Most politicians have chauffeur knowledge—memorized talking points without deep understanding.

- **"The highest form a civilization can reach is a seamless web of deserved trust"** → The ideal society has reliable people who trust each other with minimal bureaucracy.

- **"If your proposed marriage contract has 47 pages, my suggestion is you not enter"** → If you need excessive legal protection, there's no deserved trust. The same principle applies to all relationships and business.

- **"My sword I leave to him who can wear it"** → Quoting John Bunyan's Pilgrim's Progress—passing wisdom to those ready and able to use it.

## Inversion Themes (for Day 3)

### How to Guarantee Failure in Life

1. **Be Slothful and Unreliable** → If you're unreliable, your virtues don't matter. Fail to do what you've faithfully committed to do.

2. **Adopt an Extreme Ideology** → Let ideology cabbage up your mind. Become a true believer who pounds orthodox ideas into yourself without questioning.

3. **Give in to Self-Serving Bias** → Believe you're entitled to do whatever you want. Overspend your income like Mozart. Rationalize that what's good for you is good for civilization.

4. **Indulge in Self-Pity, Envy, Resentment, Revenge** → These are disastrous modes of thought. Self-pitty is close to paranoia and very hard to reverse.

5. **Work for People You Don't Admire** → Put yourself under authority figures you don't respect. We're all controlled by authority figures—choose them carefully or you'll become like them.

6. **Stay in Perverse Incentive Systems** → Let foolish incentives shape your behavior. Don't recognize that incentives control human cognition too powerfully.

7. **Stop Learning** → Believe you know enough. Don't go to bed wiser than when you woke up. You'll advance only by what you learn after leaving school, not by what you already know.

8. **Ignore Multidisciplinary Thinking** → Know things only well enough to pass exams, not to form a mental latticework. You'll end up in the middle ranks or the shallows.

9. **Surround Yourself with Chauffeur-Knowledge People** | Put responsibility in hands of those who've memorized the talk but don't really understand.

10. **Be Unprepared for Trouble** → Go through life assuming everything will go smoothly. When blows come, be submerged in self-pity rather than using them constructively.

### How to Destroy Trust

- **Don't deliver what you would buy yourself** → Sell to others what you wouldn't accept for yourself
- **Be unreliable** → Fail to keep commitments
- **Hide behind bureaucracy** → When trust is missing, rely on procedures, contracts, and lawyers instead
- **Choose partners you don't trust** → Create 47-page contracts to protect yourself instead of finding reliable people

## Psychology/Biases (for Day 5)

### Self-Serving Bias

- **Definition:** The tendency to believe that what's good for you is good for the wider civilization and that you're entitled to do what you want
- **Example:** Mozart overspending his income despite being world's greatest composer, ending up miserable
- **Countermeasure:** Recognize it in yourself AND in others. Drive it out of your thinking. Don't rationalize foolish behavior. Account for others' self-serving bias in your dealings.

### Ideological Bias (Extreme Ideology)

- **Definition:** Blind commitment to a belief system that cabbages up your mind, prevents clear thinking, leads to catastrophic decisions
- **Example:** Scandinavian canoeists who died tackling Aron Rapids because they were overconfident from easier rapids; TV preachers with minds made of cabbage
- **Countermeasure:** Don't become an unthinking loyal member of any ideology. Use the iron prescription: you're not entitled to an opinion unless you can state opposing arguments better than supporters can.

### Self-Pity (Disaster Mode)

- **Definition:** Feeling sorry for yourself when things go wrong, close to paranoia, one of hardest things to reverse
- **Example:** Standard condition in human life, but those who avoid it gain advantage
- **Countermeasure:** Train yourself out of it. View setbacks as opportunities to behave well and learn. Don't be submerged—use blows constructively like Epictetus.

### Envy, Resentment, Revenge

- **Definition:** Disastrous modes of thought that poison life and relationships
- **Example:** Common human tendencies that lead to poor decisions and unhappiness
- **Countermeasure:** Recognize these as patterns to avoid. They're counterproductive and corrosive.

### Authority Figure Control

- **Definition:** We're all subject to control by authority figures, especially those who reward us
- **Example:** Working for bosses influences behavior and thinking
- **Countermeasure:** Choose who you work for carefully. Maneuver to work only under people you admire. Become like those you serve.

## Career Applications (for Day 4)

### Choosing Employers and Mentors

- **Rule:** Work only for people you admire and want to become like
- **Why:** We're controlled by authority figures, especially those who reward us. You'll become like the people you serve.
- **Example:** Munger figured out who he admired and maneuvered cleverly without criticizing anyone to work entirely under those people.
- **Application:** Before accepting a job, ask: "Do I admire these people? Do I want to become like them?" If not, keep looking.

### Delivering Value to Clients

- **Rule:** Deliver what you would buy if you were on the other end
- **Why:** This creates deserved trust, which brings huge pleasure and long-term success
- **Application:** As a lawyer, don't just sell what clients will buy—give them what you'd accept if you were the client. Build relationships on deserved trust, not just transactions.

### Continuous Learning for Career Advancement

- **Rule:** Go to bed every night a little wiser than when you woke up
- **Why:** You advance in life by what you learn after you leave school, not by what you already know
- **Application:** Read constantly. Talk to highly gifted people you trust. Treat learning as a daily habit, not an occasional activity.

### Multidisciplinary Thinking at Work

- **Rule:** Build mental latticework of big ideas from multiple disciplines
- **Why:** Makes you more constructive, more helpful, and distinguishes you from specialists
- **Application:** Don't just know your specialty—understand psychology, economics, biology, physics. See connections others miss. But be tactful—sometimes keep your light under a bushel to avoid causing offense when you're right and others are wrong.

### Checklists for Professional Excellence

- **Rule:** Use mental checklists to apply your knowledge systematically
- **Why:** Avoids lots of errors; no other procedure works as well
- **Application:** Create checklists for important decisions (hiring, investing, client work). Run through them before committing. Elementary wisdom + checklists = powerful combination.

### Handling Career Setbacks

- **Rule:** Use every setback as an opportunity to behave well and learn
- **Why:** Some recover from blows and others don't—attitude makes the difference
- **Application:** When you're passed over, fired, or fail, don't indulge self-pity. Ask: what can I learn? How can I behave well in this situation?

### Professional Reliability

- **Rule:** Doing what you've faithfully engaged to do must be automatic
- **Why:** If you're unreliable, it doesn't matter what your virtues are
- **Application:** Meet deadlines. Keep commitments. Respond promptly. Build reputation as someone who can be counted on.

### Recognizing Perverse Incentives

- **Rule:** Avoid perverse incentive systems that cause foolish behavior
- **Why:** Incentives are too powerful a control over human cognition
- **Application:** Examine your workplace incentives. Do they reward the right behavior? If you're in a bad system, figure your way out—or at least recognize how it's affecting you.

### Earning Trust with Colleagues and Clients

- **Rule:** Build a seamless web of deserved trust
- **Why:** Highest form of professional civilization—reliable people correctly trusting each other with minimal bureaucracy
- **Application:** Be trustworthy in small things. Don't hide behind contracts and procedures. When people know they can count on you, opportunities multiply.

### Demonstrating Planck Knowledge

- **Rule:** Aim for Planck knowledge, not chauffeur knowledge
- **Why:** Deep understanding beats memorized talking points
- **Application:** Don't just repeat what you've heard. Understand fundamentals. Ask why. When you truly understand, you can solve novel problems and see what others miss.

## External Case Candidates (for Day 6)

### Historical: Salomon Brothers Scandal

**Context:** Wall Street firm faced crisis in 1991 over Treasury auction violations
**Relevance:** Munger mentions Ben Franklin's principle: "appeal to interest not to reason" when dealing with such situations
**Lesson:** Self-serving bias affects everyone. When persuading, appeal to others' interests rather than trying to reason with them.

### Historical: Mayo Clinic Operating Rooms

**Context:** Mayo Clinic's medical practice model
**Relevance:** Munger cites as example of seamless web of deserved trust
**Lesson:** High-trust environments with minimal procedure enable excellence. Reliable professionals trusting each other works better than bureaucracy.

### Contemporary: Tech Industry's "Move Fast and Break Things" Culture

**Context:** Some tech companies prioritize speed and innovation over reliability
**Relevance:** Contrast to Munger's emphasis on reliability and avoiding sloth
**Lesson:** Being unreliable doesn't work long-term, no matter how smart or fast you are. Break things, and you break trust.

### Contemporary: Corporate Bloat and Legal Departments

**Context:** Companies with extensive contracts, legal protections, and bureaucracy
**Relevance:** Contrast to seamless web of deserved trust—Munger's 47-page marriage contract analogy
**Lesson:** When trust is deserved, you don't need 47-page contracts. Excessive legal protection signals lack of trust or reliability.

### Personal: The Employee Who Chose the Wrong Boss

**Context:** Someone accepts a job with high salary but works for an unprincipled boss they don't admire
**Relevance:** Munger's warning about perverse associations and authority figures
**Lesson:** You become like those you serve. Even high compensation doesn't offset becoming someone you don't respect.

### Personal: The Lawyer Who Wins Arguments but Loses Clients

**Context:** A brilliant lawyer who always wins in court but clients don't return
**Relevance:** Munger's advice to sometimes keep your light under a bushel to avoid causing offense
**Lesson:** Being right in a way that causes others to lose face is dangerous. Tact matters as much as correctness.

### Berkshire: Berkshire Hathaway's Minimal Bureaucracy

**Context:** Berkshire's simple corporate structure with few rules, lots of autonomy for managers
**Relevance:** Seamless web of deserved trust in action
**Lesson:** When you have trustworthy people running businesses, you don't need complex procedures. Trust + autonomy = effectiveness.

### Berkshire: Munger and Buffett's Relationship

**Context:** Decades of partnership with minimal legal agreements, maximum trust
**Relevance:** Model of seamless web of deserved trust
**Lesson:** Their 47-page "marriage" would have been one page: "I trust you, you trust me, let's build something great."

## Research Notes

This is a later addition to Poor Charlie's Almanack, appearing in the expanded third edition. It was given when Munger was 84, offering late-career wisdom that synthesizes themes from earlier talks (multidisciplinary thinking, inversion, psychology) but focuses specifically on personal conduct and character for entering professionals.

The talk is remarkably practical and actionable, offering 18 specific principles for life success. Unlike more theoretical talks (Academic Economics, Practical Thought), this one is pure applied wisdom—character traits, habits, and choices anyone can implement.

Key insight: The talk emphasizes both individual virtue (deserving what you want, learning continuously) and systemic awareness (avoid perverse incentives, work for admirable people). Success requires both personal excellence and wise environment selection.

Munger's personal anecdotes (grandfather's prudence, his own anticipation of trouble) make this more intimate than other talks. He's not just teaching—he's showing how he lived.
