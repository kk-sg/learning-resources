# Master Notes: Talk 7 – Philanthropy Roundtable

## Talk Context

- **When:** November 10, 2000
- **Where:** Breakfast Meeting of the Philanthropy Roundtable, Washington, D.C.
- **Why:** Munger argues that economists underestimate how rising stock prices influence spending and economic growth via the wealth effect; he explains how institutional practices (fees, stock option accounting, tax, wealth effects, and pension accounting) create “febezzlement” (functional embezzlement) and how foundations can stop destroying charitable capital.

## Core Argument

Wealth effects from rising prices drive consumption and investment, and a large part of this effect is “febezzlement” (wealth stripped by avoidable waste). Foundations, by adopting costly, complex consultant structures and ignoring how fees, accounting, tax, and pensions compound the wealth effect, destroy charitable capital. To stop this, they must eliminate waste, simplify structures, and model responsible stewardship.

## Structure Outline

1. Wealth Effect and Its Drivers
   - How rising stock prices increase reported earnings (pension accounting), and thus prices
   - How consumption/spending proclivity tracks with rising/falling prices
2. Febezzlement Defined
   - The functional equivalent of embezzlement (fees, accounting distortions, self-delusion)
   - Foundation 3% annual drag as febezzle
   - Employee stock options as febezzle (corrupt accounting of expensing)
3. Economist Blind Spots
   - Overlooking bezzle/febezzle in macro models
   - Overvaluing “rational man” vs. “foolish man” psychology
   - Underestimating long-term “virtue effects”
4. Systemic Incentives & Feedback Loops
   - Reputation risk driving “fail conventionally”
   - Complexity that mimics sophistication but adds waste
   - The Jody Curtis characterization: “friendly old uncle with jolly humor”
5. Investment Practices and Capital Preservation
   - Incentive-driven overconfidence, layered consultants, social proof
   - Arithmetic: cost drag vs. realistic returns
   - Berkshire vs. conventional foundation structures
6. Philanthropy as Stewardship
   - Foundations must reduce waste and be role models
   - Ethical accounting matters (virtue/vice effects long term)
   - Munger’s personal giving: education, local institutions, concentration on few causes

## Key Examples/Case Studies (from Munger)

1. **Foundations’ 3% annual cost drag**
   - Layered consultants add 3% in fees/friction; compounded, this shrinks capital especially in low-return periods.
2. **Employee stock options ($750B “floating, ever-growing” wealth)**
   - Accounting failure to expense creates a febezzle effect and feeds the stock-price wealth effect.
3. **Pension accounting and stock prices**
   - Rising prices reduce pension cost accruals, boosting reported earnings, which in turn push prices higher (feedback loop).
4. **Japan’s experience with reverse wealth effects**
   - Falling prices and spending confirm that wealth effects work in both directions; academic economics should heed this.
5. **Friend’s mutual fund data**
   - Funds earn ~12.8% annually, but investors receive ~7.25% after fees/friction (noted in source commentary).
6. **Keynes on reputation**
   - “Better for reputation to fail conventionally than to succeed unconventionally” (paraphrased; applied to professional behavior).
7. **GM fourth door mistake**
   - Professionalism trumping observation and common sense; parallels overconfidence/complexity in investing.
8. **LTCM collapse**
   - Overconfidence + sophistication + leverage + lollapalooza effects in a disaster.
9. **Woodruff Foundation’s Coca-Cola concentration**
   - Example of rational concentration when understanding runs deep.
10. **Berkshire’s low-cost approach**
    - Costs <0.1%, zero turnover, few positions, deep understanding; beats complex consultant structures.

## Quotes to Paraphrase

- “I just want to know where I’m going to die so I can never go there” → Inversion: Identify failure modes and avoid them.
- “Show me the incentive and I’ll show you the outcome” → Incentives drive behavior; design them wisely.
- “To the man with only a hammer, every problem looks like a nail” → Man-with-a-Hammer Syndrome; avoid single-model thinking.
- Keynes’ “better for reputation to fail conventionally than to succeed unconventionally” → Reputation bias drives conformist, wasteful structures.

## Inversion Themes (for Day 3)

- How to destroy foundation wealth: 3% annual cost drag + complex layered consultants + high turnover + social proof copying + overconfidence + no self-criticism.
- How to guarantee “febezzlement”: Hire layered consultants, ignore total costs, avoid self-criticism, chase convention, use corrupt accounting, leverage excessively.
- How to fail in philanthropy: Don’t measure outcomes, copy what others do, ignore incentives, embrace complexity, spend capital on inefficiencies.

## Psychology/Biases (for Day 5)

- Incentive-Caused Bias → Three-layer consultants maximize their fees; foundations suffer.
- Overconfidence Bias → 90% of Swedish drivers rate above average; professionals overestimate their edge.
- Social Proof → Foundations copy Yale’s complex structures because others do it.
- Lollapalooza Effect → Incentives + overconfidence + social proof + complexity combine to destroy capital.
- Man-with-a-Hammer → Using only one professional lens (e.g., consultants’ frameworks) to manage investments.
- Reputation Bias (“fail conventionally”) → Professionals avoid unconventional but superior approaches.

## Career Applications (for Day 4)

- Evaluate incentive structures before accepting roles.
- Avoid copying complexity because “everyone does it”.
- Measure total costs and their long-term impact; eliminate waste.
- Build incentives aligned with outcomes, not activities.
- Use inversion to identify failure paths in work/projects.

## External Case Candidates (for Day 6)

- Historical: LTCM (overconfidence + leverage + lollapalooza)
- Contemporary: High-fee mutual/fund-of-funds structures delivering low net returns to investors
- Personal: Workplace systems designed for professional appearances rather than outcomes
- Berkshire: Low-cost, concentrated holdings vs. complex consultant layers
