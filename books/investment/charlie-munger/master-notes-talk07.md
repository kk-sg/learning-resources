# Master Notes: Talk 7 – Philanthropy Roundtable

## Talk Context

- **When:** November 10, 2000
- **Where:** Breakfast Meeting of the Philanthropy Roundtable, Washington, D.C.
- **Why:** Munger wanted to help large foundations make fewer mistakes in investment management and reduce waste. This was a follow-up to his earlier talk on charitable foundation investment practices, expanding on the economic consequences of poor management.

## Core Argument

Foundations and institutional investors engage in "febezzlement" — functional embezzlement created by unnecessary investment costs and lemming-like behavior — which systematically destroys charitable capital while creating illusory wealth effects.

## Key Examples/Case Studies (from Munger)

### 1. The 3% Foundation Annual Drain
- **Context:** Foundations hire layers of consultants who take ~3% annually in fees
- **Munger's calculation:** In good times (17% returns): 17% - 3% costs - 5% donations = 9% net growth. In bad times (5% returns): 5% - 3% costs - 5% donations = -3% shrinkage
- **Insight:** The croupier always gets paid, whether foundation grows or shrinks

### 2. Mutual Fund Performance Gap
- **Context:** Munger's friend provided data
- **The gap:** Mutual funds earn 12.8% annual returns, but investors receive only 7.25%
- **The waste:** 5.55% difference goes to fees and frictional costs
- **Insight:** Febezzle works because investors "feel richer" while actually being drained

### 3. Pension Cost Accrual Reduction
- **Context:** How rising stock prices affect corporate earnings
- **Mechanism:** Companies reduce pension cost accruals when stock prices rise → earnings increase → stock prices rise more
- **Insight:** Self-reinforcing wealth effect cycle

### 4. Lemming-Like Institutional Behavior
- **Context:** Foundation investment practices
- **Problem:** "Each institutional investor apparently fears most of all that its investment practices will be different from the practices of the rest of the crowd"
- **Insight:** Better to fail conventionally than succeed unconventionally (Keynes quote)

### 5. "Foolish Man" vs. "Rational Man" Models
- **Context:** Economists' modeling choices
- **Problem:** Economists use "rational man" models instead of "foolish man" models from psychology
- **Insight:** Real-world behavior is better predicted by psychological tendencies than economic rationality

## Inversion Themes (for Day 3)

### How to Guarantee Foundation Wealth Destruction

1. **Hire three layers of consultants** → Each layer takes ~1% fees, creating 3% annual drain
2. **Copy whatever other foundations do** → Lemming behavior ensures you make everyone else's mistakes
3. **Believe in "rational man" models** → Ignore psychological biases that actually drive behavior
4. **Feel rich when markets rise** → Spend based on illusory wealth effects from febezzle
5. **Ignore long-term virtue effects** → Assume short-term gains won't have consequences

### How to Create Economic Misery

1. **Design systems that hide costs** → Febezzle works because waste is disguised
2. **Normalize institutional copycat behavior** → Groupthink prevents innovation
3. **Reward conventional failure** → Social proof protects mediocrity
4. **Underestimate psychological factors** → Economic models ignore real human behavior
5. **Pretend rising markets equal real wealth** → Wealth effects create illusion without substance

## Psychology/Biases (for Day 5)

1. **Social Proof / Lemming Effect** → Foundations copy each other to avoid standing out
2. **Incentive-Caused Bias** → Consultants have incentive to increase complexity and fees
3. **Inconsistency-Avoidance** → Once a foundation adopts a model, it's hard to change
4. **Availability Bias** → Rising markets make "wealth feel real" despite waste
5. **Excessive Self-Regard** → Foundation boards believe they're making rational decisions
6. **Denial** → Hard to acknowledge that 3% annual waste is destroying charitable mission
7. **Contrast Effect** → Compare themselves to other wasteful foundations instead to efficient models

## Career Applications (for Day 4)

### For Investment Professionals

1. **Design incentive-aligned fee structures** → Charge based on net results, not assets under management
2. **Minimize transaction costs** → Fewer trades = less waste
3. **Avoid layering consultants** → Simplify decision-making
4. **Measure true economic returns** → Subtract all costs, not just explicit fees

### For Foundation Boards

1. **Audit total investment costs** → Include consultant fees, transaction costs, opportunity costs
2. **Question "industry standard" practices** → Just because everyone does it doesn't mean it's smart
3. **Consider Berkshire-style concentration** → Fewer positions, deeper understanding, lower costs
4. **Track net returns, not gross** → 17% gross with 3% costs = 9% net. Be honest about what you keep

### For Individual Investors

1. **Watch for febezzle in your own portfolio** → Are you paying hidden fees?
2. **Avoid herd behavior** → Don't buy just because everyone else is
3. **Calculate true costs** → Include expense ratios, transaction costs, tax drag
4. **Focus on net, not gross** → What you keep matters more than what you earn

### For Any Professional

1. **Avoid "leverage-like" febezzle** → Systems that feel good but drain resources
2. **Question rationality assumptions** → Real people aren't perfectly rational
3. **Measure outcomes, not outputs** → Did you create value, or just activity?
4. **Beware virtue effects** → Short-term gains may have long-term costs

## External Case Candidates (for Day 6)

### Historical Business Case: The Febezzle of Mutual Funds

- **Context:** Mutual funds earn 12.8% annually, but investors receive only 7.25%
- **Mistake:** 5.55% difference goes to fees and frictional costs
- **Relevant Talk Idea:** Febezzle — functional embezzlement disguised as earned income
- **Micro-Lesson:** The gap between gross and net returns is where wealth is destroyed

### Contemporary Example: Private Equity Fee Structures

- **Context:** "2 and 20" fee model creates substantial cost drag
- **Mistake:** Many funds underperform benchmarks after fees
- **Relevant Talk Idea:** Febezzle continues because fees are rationalized as "earned"
- **Micro-Lesson:** Always ask: "What do I keep, not what I earn?"

### Personal/Relatable: The Coffee Subscription Febezzle

- **Context:** Paying $30/month for coffee you drink 5 times = $6/cup
- **Mistake:** Illusion of savings through "membership" while waste accumulates
- **Relevant Talk Idea:** Small recurring costs add up to significant febezzle
- **Micro-Lesson:** Track real cost-per-use, not membership cost

### Berkshire Example: Berkshire's Low-Cost Approach

- **Context:** Berkshire's investment costs <0.1% annually
- **Success:** Avoids febezzle through concentration and low turnover
- **Relevant Talk Idea:** Simplicity beats complexity; low costs preserve wealth
- **Micro-Lesson:** In investing, what you don't pay is as important as what you earn

### Inversion Case: The "Fund of Funds" Disaster

- **Context:** Foundations invest in funds of funds → double layer of fees
- **Mistake:** Total costs can exceed 4% annually
- **Relevant Talk Idea:** Febezzle through consultant layering
- **Micro-Lesson:** Simplicity: Invest directly in assets or simple index funds

**Good Times:**
- Gross return: 17%

**Bad Times:**
- Gross return: 5%

- Starting with $100, losing 3% annually → $55 remaining (45% destroyed)

### Mutual Fund Investor Gap
- Fund return: 12.8%
- Investor return: 7.25%
- **Febezzle: 5.55% annually**

### Berkshire vs. Conventional Foundation

**Conventional (3% costs):**
- Good markets: 17% - 3% - 5% = 9%
- Bad markets: 5% - 3% - 5% = -3%

- Bad markets: 5% - 0.1% - 5% = -0.1%

**Berkshire advantage:** 2.9% more growth annually in good markets; 2.9% less shrinkage in bad markets

## Core Frameworks

### 1. Febezzle Framework

**Definition:** Functional equivalent of embezzlement created by unnecessary costs, disguised as earned income.

**Key characteristics:**
- Not criminal (unlike actual bezzle)
- Self-reinforcing (can continue for years)
- Creates illusion of wealth while draining resources
- Amplified by wealth effects and Keynes multiplier

**Examples:**
- Foundation investment costs (3% annual drain)
- Mutual fund fee gap (5.55% difference)
- High-turnover trading costs

**Countermeasure:** Calculate total costs, measure net returns, simplify structures

---

### 2. Wealth Effect Cycle

**Mechanism:**
1. Stock prices rise
2. Corporate earnings increase (e.g., pension cost accrual reductions)
3. Stock prices rise further
4. Spending increases (wealth effect)
5. Stock prices rise more
6. Cycle reinforces

**Munger's insight:** This cycle creates illusory wealth, especially when febezzle is hidden

**Countermeasure:** Look past nominal prices to real economic value

---

### 3. Rational Man vs. Foolish Man Models

**Rational Man (Economics):**
- Assumes perfect rationality
- Ignores psychological biases
- Predicts optimal decisions

**Foolish Man (Psychology/Reality):**
- Recognizes cognitive limitations
- Accounts for incentives, biases, social proof
- Predicts actual behavior

**Munger's critique:** Economists rely too much on rational man models and ignore foolish man realities

**Countermeasure:** Use multidisciplinary thinking; incorporate psychology into economic analysis

## Research Notes

This talk extends Munger's critique of foundation investment practices (Talk 6) by introducing the broader economic concept of febezzlement. While Talk 6 focuses on the mechanics of the 3% cost problem and layered consultants, Talk 7 explores the economic consequences — how febezzle creates wealth effects, how it relates to Keynesian multipliers, and how it persists due to psychological biases.

Key connection to Talk 9 (Academic Economics): Both talks criticize economists' over-reliance on "rational man" models and failure to incorporate psychological realities. Munger argues that "foolish man" models from psychology are more predictive of real-world behavior.

The concept of febezzlement is one of Munger's most original contributions to economic thinking — a term he coined to describe a widespread phenomenon that economists ignore but that systematically destroys wealth across the investment industry.
