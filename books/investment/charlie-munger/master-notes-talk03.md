# Master Notes: Talk 3 – A Lesson on Elementary, Worldly Wisdom, Revisited

## Talk Context

- **When:** April 19, 1996
- **Where:** Stanford Law School
- **Why:** A follow-up to Talk 2 (USC Business School, 1994). Munger revisits the latticework of mental models concept, but this time directs his advice specifically to law students and legal professionals. He deepens the psychology dimension, introduces new moral/ethical dimensions tied to worldly wisdom, and adds practical investment and career advice through an extended Q&A session.

## Core Argument

Worldly wisdom is mostly simple but requires you to break free from single-discipline thinking and ideological blinders. Psychology is the most important missing model in most people's toolkit. Morality is deeply entwined with worldly wisdom — bad incentive systems corrupt people, and creating systems that reward cheating causes cascading ruin.

## Structure Outline

1. **Recap of the Latticework Concept** — Revisits the need for mental models from multiple disciplines, emphasising that the world is not organized by academic departments
2. **Psychology as the Critical Missing Model** — The most important models people lack are in psychology; covers ideological bias, denial, incentive-caused bias, social proof, consistency principle, operant conditioning
3. **The Chomsky–Pinker Example (Ideological Bias)** — How even a genius like Noam Chomsky can be blinded by ideology; heavy ideology distorts cognition
4. **Morality and Psychology Are Entwined** — Stealing, cheating, and the design of systems that either prevent or reward dishonesty
5. **Captain Cook's Sauerkraut Story** — Constructive use of psychology (social proof + authority bias) to get sailors to eat vitamin C
6. **Incentive Systems and Fraud** — Workers' compensation fraud, the Serpico effect, crooked systems creating crooked people
7. **The Helmet Business / Deep Pocket Problem** — Some businesses are structurally dangerous because of litigation risk
8. **The Navy Model of Accountability** — No-excuse accountability creates better outcomes
9. **Investment Philosophy (Q&A)** — One-foot fences, pari-mutuel thinking, mispriced opportunities, changing your mind
10. **Handling Mistakes** — Psychological denial, sunk cost, consistency principle causing people to go broke
11. **The Grant McFayden Lesson** — Run your life like the honest, sensible client, not the walking minefield
12. **Career and Professional Advice** — Don't pretend to know what you don't; admit ignorance; appeal to interests not morals when persuading

## Mental Models Mentioned

1. **Latticework of Mental Models** — (recap from Talk 2) Multiple models from multiple disciplines
2. **Ideological Bias / Heavy Ideology** — Ideology distorts cognition; pounds ideas in better than it convinces out
3. **Psychological Denial** — Miscognition from denial pervades reality; people refuse to see what's painful
4. **Incentive-Caused Bias** — People rationalise bad behaviour when incentives push them toward it
5. **Consistency Principle** — Once people start something (stealing, investing, etc.), they feel compelled to continue
6. **Operant Conditioning** — Behaviour followed by reward increases; creates habit loops
7. **Social Proof** — When enough people do wrong, they turn on whistleblowers (Serpico effect)
8. **Pavlovian Association** — People hate the messenger who brings bad news
9. **Authority Bias / Social Influence** — Captain Cook's officers eating sauerkraut first to influence the crew
10. **Sunk Cost Fallacy** — Pouring more into failing ventures because of prior commitment
11. **Deprival Super-Reaction Syndrome** — Fear of losing what you've invested drives irrational continuation
12. **Circle of Competence** — Don't answer questions outside your expertise; Jack Welch / Bee analogy
13. **Pari-Mutuel / Mispricing** — Investment is about finding mispriced opportunities with favourable odds
14. **Inversion** — Think forward and backward; "how would I fail?"
15. **Compound Interest** — One of the most important models on earth
16. **No-Excuse Accountability (Navy Model)** — Systems with strict accountability work better
17. **Appeal to Interest (not morality)** — Persuasion works through self-interest, not moral lectures
