# Day 13: The Mom Test
## *How to Talk to Customers & Learn If Your Business Is a Good Idea When Everyone Is Lying to You*
### by Rob Fitzpatrick

---

# 📚 Book Overview

**"The Mom Test"** is a practical guide that teaches you how to have honest conversations with potential users and stakeholders—even when everyone wants to be nice to you. The title comes from a simple truth: your mom will always say your idea is great because she loves you. But in instructional design, e-learning development, and AI implementation, polite lies can lead to months of wasted effort building solutions nobody wants.

Rob Fitzpatrick wrote this book from his frustrations as an introverted entrepreneur trying to figure out how to do effective customer development. The result is a deceptively simple framework that transforms how you gather requirements, conduct needs analysis, and validate learning solutions.

## Why This Book Matters for Instructional Designers

In L&D, we face the same problem Fitzpatrick describes:
- **Stakeholders say "yes" to everything** because they don't want to disappoint us
- **Learners give polite feedback** on training that doesn't actually help them
- **Executives approve projects** based on enthusiasm, not evidence
- **Subject matter experts** tell us what they *think* learners need, not what learners actually struggle with

The Mom Test gives you tools to cut through the noise and discover what your learners and stakeholders *actually* need—not what they think you want to hear.

# 🧠 Core Framework: The Mom Test Rules

## The Three Rules of The Mom Test

### Rule 1: Talk About Their Life, Not Your Idea

> "You learn nothing useful when you talk about your idea." — Rob Fitzpatrick

The moment you mention your course concept, LMS feature, or AI tool, people switch into "be nice" mode. They stop sharing their real problems and start giving you compliments.

**What NOT to do:**
- "I'm thinking of creating a microlearning series on leadership. What do you think?"
- "Would you use an AI-powered coaching platform?"
- "Do you think this e-learning module would be helpful?"

**What TO do:**
- "Walk me through the last time you had to give difficult feedback to a team member."
- "How do you currently handle leadership development conversations?"
- "Tell me about the tools you've tried for coaching. What worked? What didn't?"

### Rule 2: Ask About Specifics in the Past, Not Hypotheticals in the Future

> "Compliments are dangerous, and details are gold." — Rob Fitzpatrick

People are terrible at predicting their future behavior. They'll say they'd "definitely" complete a 10-hour course, but actual completion rates tell a different story. Past behavior is the best predictor of future behavior.

**Bad Questions (Hypotheticals):**
- "Would you take this course if we offered it?"
- "How much time would you spend on learning each week?"
- "Would an AI tutor help you learn better?"

**Good Questions (Past Specifics):**
- "When was the last time you completed a professional development course? What motivated you?"
- "Tell me about the last time you set aside time for learning. What happened?"
- "Have you ever used an AI tool for learning? Walk me through that experience."

### Rule 3: Listen More Than You Talk

> "Embrace pauses and silence, don't fill every gap with your own pitch." — Rob Fitzpatrick

When you talk, you bias the conversation. When you stay quiet, people fill the silence with real insights. This is especially hard for instructional designers who love explaining their solutions.

**The 80/20 Rule:** In customer conversations, the other person should talk 80% of the time.

**Techniques:**
- Ask a question, then count to 5 before speaking again
- When someone finishes, ask "What else?" or "Tell me more"
- Take notes—it keeps you quiet and shows you're listening

# 📖 Chapter-by-Chapter Insights

## Chapter 1: The Problem with Customer Conversations

**Key Insight:** Everyone lies to you, but not maliciously. They lie because they want to be nice, because they don't want to hurt your feelings, because they want to encourage you.

**For L&D:** When you ask stakeholders "Do you think this training will work?" they'll say yes. When you ask learners "Would this course be helpful?" they'll say yes. But these are lies of kindness that lead to failed projects.

**Application:**
- Stop asking for opinions on your training ideas
- Start asking about problems they've actually experienced
- Look for evidence of pain (workarounds, complaints, lost productivity)

## Chapter 2: The Three Types of Misleading Data

Fitzpatrick identifies three types of bad data that masquerade as useful feedback:

### 1. Compliments
**What it sounds like:** "That's a great idea!" "I love that concept!" "This sounds really useful!"

**Why it's dangerous:** Compliments feel good but provide zero actionable information. They often come from people trying to be supportive, not from genuine interest.

**How to handle it:** Deflect with curiosity. "Thanks, but I'm more interested in understanding your current challenges. Can you tell me about a time when..."

### 2. Fluff (Generic Claims)
**What it sounds like:** "I usually..." "I always..." "I never..." "I would definitely..."

**Why it's dangerous:** These are opinions disguised as facts. "I usually complete online courses" is very different from "I completed 3 out of 12 assigned courses last quarter."

**How to handle it:** Anchor to specifics. "When you say 'usually,' can you give me a specific example from the last month?"

### 3. Ideas (Feature Requests)
**What it sounds like:** "You should add gamification!" "What if it had AI chat?" "Have you considered video?"

**Why it's dangerous:** People are great at imagining features but terrible at predicting what they'll actually use. Feature requests distract from understanding real problems.

**How to handle it:** Dig into motivations. "That's interesting—what problem would that solve for you? Have you tried solving that problem before?"

## Chapter 3: Asking Important Questions

**Key Insight:** Pre-plan the 3 most important things you want to learn from any conversation.

**Framework for L&D Needs Analysis:**

Before any stakeholder or learner interview, identify:
1. **What problem am I trying to understand?**
2. **What evidence would prove this problem exists?**
3. **What would disprove my assumptions?**

**Sample Learning-Specific Questions:**
- "Walk me through the last time [skill gap] caused a problem at work."
- "What have you tried to solve this? What happened?"
- "How much time/money/productivity does this problem cost you?"
- "Who else is affected by this problem?"
- "What would success look like? How would you measure it?"

## Chapter 4: The Rules in Action

**Key Insight:** The less formal the conversation, the more honest the feedback.

**For E-learning:**
- Coffee chats beat formal meetings
- Casual conversations at conferences beat scheduled interviews
- Observing real work beats asking about work
- Shadowing sessions beat surveys

**Pro Tips:**
- Record conversations when possible (with permission)
- Take verbatim notes—exact quotes matter
- Have a teammate take notes so you can focus on listening
- Write up notes immediately after, before memory fades

## Chapter 5: Finding Conversations

**Key Insight:** You only need one "yes" to start. One good conversation leads to warm introductions to three more people.

**Channels for L&D User Research:**
- Internal: managers, recent hires, top performers, struggling employees
- External: industry associations, LinkedIn groups, conference attendees
- SMEs: those who've trained others, those who've received training
- Leadership: sponsors of previous learning initiatives

**The "Research" Frame:**
Reach out by saying you're "doing research" on [topic]. People love to share expertise. They hate to be sold to.

## Chapter 6: Choosing Your Market

**Key Insight:** Before you can serve everyone, you have to serve someone. Customer segmentation is your friend.

**For L&D:** Don't try to create "training that works for everyone." Start with a specific audience:
- New managers in their first 90 days
- Customer service reps handling escalated calls
- Sales teams pitching a new product line
- Engineers learning a new programming language

**The Narrower, The Better:**
"Customer service reps" is too broad.
"B2B SaaS customer service reps handling technical support escalations for enterprise clients" is specific enough to learn deeply.

## Chapter 7: Analyzing What You've Learned

**Key Insight:** You know you've had enough conversations when you can predict what the next person will say.

**Signs You're Learning:**
- You hear the same problems repeatedly
- You can finish people's sentences (accurately)
- You've stopped being surprised by answers
- Patterns emerge across different people

**Signs You're NOT Learning:**
- Every conversation feels different
- You keep hearing new problems
- You can't predict the next answer
- No clear patterns emerge

## Chapter 8: Getting to Commitment

**Key Insight:** A meeting has succeeded when it ends with a commitment to advance to the next step.

**Types of Commitment (in order of strength):**

1. **Reputation** (weakest): Introducing you to someone else
2. **Time**: Scheduling a follow-up meeting, doing homework
3. **Risk**: Using their name, giving a testimonial
4. **Money** (strongest): Pre-ordering, paying a deposit

**For L&D Projects:**
- Ask stakeholders to commit budget
- Ask learners to commit time (pilot program signup)
- Ask managers to commit employee hours
- Ask SMEs to commit review sessions

# 🛠️ Practical Frameworks with Steps

## Framework 1: The Learning Needs Mom Test

Use this framework when conducting needs analysis for any learning intervention.

### Step 1: Identify Your Learning Segment
Be specific about who you're designing for:
- Role/level
- Industry/context
- Experience level
- Specific challenges

### Step 2: Pre-Plan Your 3 Big Questions
What do you absolutely need to learn? Examples:
1. "What's the cost of this skill gap in productivity/errors/lost revenue?"
2. "What have they tried before? Why didn't it work?"
3. "What would success look like? How would we measure it?"

### Step 3: Find 10 People in Your Segment
Use warm introductions, internal networks, industry groups.

### Step 4: Conduct Casual Conversations
- Don't mention your training idea
- Ask about their life/work/problems
- Focus on past specifics, not future hypotheticals
- Listen 80%, talk 20%

### Step 5: Look for Patterns
After 5-10 conversations, patterns should emerge:
- Common problems with similar symptoms
- Similar failed solutions
- Similar success criteria

### Step 6: Test Commitment
Ask for something: "Would you be willing to spend 30 minutes testing an early prototype?"

## Framework 2: The Stakeholder Conversation Guide

Use this when interviewing project sponsors and stakeholders.

### Opening (2 minutes)
"Thanks for meeting. I'm trying to understand [problem area] better. I won't waste your time—I have a few specific questions, and I'd love to learn from your experience."

### The Problem Exploration (10 minutes)
- "Walk me through the last time [problem] affected your team."
- "What happened? What was the impact?"
- "How did people handle it? What workarounds developed?"

### Past Solutions (5 minutes)
- "What training or solutions have you tried before?"
- "What worked? What didn't?"
- "Why do you think that approach succeeded/failed?"

### Success Criteria (3 minutes)
- "If we solved this perfectly, what would be different?"
- "How would you know it worked?"
- "What would you see happening differently?"

### The Commitment Ask (2 minutes)
"I'm exploring some potential solutions. Would you be willing to [commitment: review an outline / provide pilot users / allocate budget]?"

---

## Framework 3: The Learner Interview Protocol

Use this when talking directly to potential learners.

### Opening
"I'm not selling anything—I'm just trying to understand how [job area] works for you. Your honest perspective would be really helpful."

### Context Setting
- "Tell me about your role. What are you responsible for?"
- "What does a typical day look like?"
- "What takes up most of your time?"

### Problem Discovery
- "What's the hardest part about [skill area]?"
- "Tell me about the last time you struggled with [task]."
- "Walk me through what happened step by step."

### Current Solutions
- "How do you currently handle [challenge]?"
- "What tools or resources do you use?"
- "What's missing from your current approach?"

### Evidence of Pain
- "How often does this problem come up?"
- "What's the impact when it happens?"
- "Have you looked for solutions? What did you find?"

### Learning Behavior
- "When you need to learn something new for work, what do you do?"
- "Tell me about the last work-related thing you learned. How did that happen?"
- "What makes you decide to invest time in learning something?"

---

## Framework 4: The AI Learning Solution Validation Framework

Use this when considering AI-powered learning solutions.

### Validation Questions for AI Features:

**Don't Ask:** "Would you use an AI tutor?"
**Do Ask:** "Have you ever used ChatGPT or similar AI tools for learning? Walk me through that."

**Don't Ask:** "Would adaptive learning be helpful?"
**Do Ask:** "Tell me about a time when training was either too basic or too advanced for you. What happened?"

**Don't Ask:** "Would you like personalized learning paths?"
**Do Ask:** "When you've learned something new, have you ever wished it was tailored to your specific situation? Tell me about that."

### AI-Specific Evidence to Look For:
- Have they used AI tools unprompted?
- Do they actively seek out AI solutions?
- What AI tools have they tried? What worked/failed?
- What's their actual AI usage behavior vs. stated preferences?

# 🏢 Five Case Studies: E-Learning & AI Applications

## Case Study 1: The Leadership Training That Wasn't Needed

**The Situation:** An L&D team was asked to create a comprehensive leadership development program for first-time managers. The budget was approved, the timeline was set, and development had begun.

**The Mom Test Application:** Before continuing, the ID team conducted informal conversations with 15 new managers using Mom Test principles. They didn't mention the training program—they just asked about challenges.

**What They Discovered:**
- New managers didn't struggle with leadership *concepts*
- They struggled with *company-specific processes* for approvals, budgets, and HR
- The "leadership problems" HR reported were actually *system navigation problems*
- Managers had already created informal peer support networks

**The Pivot:** Instead of a leadership program, they created:
- A quick-reference guide for company processes
- A peer mentoring matching system
- Monthly informal "new manager" coffee chats

**The AI Angle:** The team considered building an AI assistant to answer process questions, but their Mom Test conversations revealed managers preferred asking humans because it built relationships.

**Outcome:** 80% cost reduction, higher satisfaction scores, faster time-to-productivity.

---

## Case Study 2: The Compliance Training That Learners Actually Completed

**The Situation:** Annual compliance training had 45% completion rates. The team was asked to "make it more engaging" with gamification and microlearning.

**The Mom Test Application:** Instead of assuming engagement was the problem, the team interviewed 25 learners about compliance training—without mentioning their improvement plans.

**What They Discovered:**
- Completion wasn't about engagement—it was about *scheduling*
- Learners couldn't find 90-minute blocks during busy periods
- Many didn't know the deadline until the reminder email
- The "I'll do it later" problem was actually an "I don't know when" problem
- People who completed it early did so during slow periods (predictable)

**The Pivot:** Instead of gamification:
- Calendar integration that auto-suggested 15-minute blocks
- AI that identified low-workload periods and sent "do it now" nudges
- Split training into 15-minute modules that could be done anytime
- Deadline awareness campaign 60 days in advance

**The AI Angle:** An AI assistant analyzed calendar patterns and workload data to suggest optimal completion windows for each employee.

**Outcome:** Completion rates increased to 94%. "Engagement" features weren't needed.

---

## Case Study 3: The AI-Powered Sales Enablement Platform

**The Situation:** A sales enablement team wanted to build an AI coaching platform that would analyze sales calls and provide real-time feedback.

**The Mom Test Application:** The team interviewed 20 sales reps, asking about their call preparation and review processes—without mentioning the AI platform.

**What They Discovered:**
- Top performers *never* reviewed their own call recordings (too painful, too long)
- Struggling reps *wanted* to review calls but didn't know what to look for
- Managers reviewed calls only for deals that were in trouble
- Everyone wanted *peer examples* of good calls—not AI feedback on their own
- The real problem: finding relevant examples of successful calls

**The Pivot:** Instead of AI feedback on every call:
- AI that identifies and tags "golden moments" in successful calls
- Searchable library of exemplar call snippets by objection type
- Manager dashboard highlighting coaching opportunities
- Peer recognition system for great call moments

**The AI Angle:** The AI didn't coach—it *curated*. It found the needle-in-a-haystack moments that reps actually wanted to learn from.

**Outcome:** Platform adoption 3x higher than projected. Reps voluntarily used it daily.

## Case Study 4: The Customer Service Learning Path Nobody Used

**The Situation:** An e-learning company built a 40-hour customer service learning path with 12 modules, quizzes, and a final certification. Completion rates were 12%.

**The Mom Test Application:** The team interviewed both completers and non-completers about their learning habits—without mentioning the learning path redesign.

**What They Discovered:**
- Non-completers weren't lazy—they were *busy*
- The average time between customer contacts was 90 seconds
- Learning had to happen *between calls*—there was no other time
- Completers had done it during a slow week or had been assigned dedicated training time
- The real need: quick answers to questions that came up *during* calls

**The Pivot:** Instead of a comprehensive curriculum:
- AI-powered "instant answer" bot for common customer questions
- 2-minute "just-in-time" microlearning for specific scenarios
- Weekly 5-minute "tip of the week" delivered during shift start
- Peer-contributed "how I handled it" video snippets (30 seconds each)

**The AI Angle:** An AI assistant integrated with the phone system could detect call topics and serve relevant tips in real-time.

**Outcome:** Voluntary learning engagement increased from 12% to 67%. Customer satisfaction scores improved 23%.

## Case Study 5: The Onboarding Program Built on False Assumptions

**The Situation:** A tech company's onboarding program covered company history, product overview, and culture. New hire feedback was positive, but 6-month retention was only 68%.

**The Mom Test Application:** The L&D team interviewed people who had left within 6 months, plus successful 1-year employees. They didn't ask about onboarding—they asked about the first 90 days.

**What They Discovered:**
- Company history and culture content was "nice" but not impactful
- New hires felt productive after month 3, but the first 3 months were confusing
- The "real" onboarding happened through informal peer relationships
- People who left cited: "I didn't know what success looked like" and "I felt like I was bothering people with questions"
- Successful employees had identified a "go-to person" within their first week

**The Pivot:** Instead of content-heavy onboarding:
- "First Week Mission": Find and interview 5 people about their roles
- "30-Day Challenge": Complete one real task with peer support
- AI-powered FAQ bot trained on common new hire questions
- Structured "onboarding buddy" program with specific check-in milestones
- Clear "success criteria" document for first 30/60/90 days

**The AI Angle:** An AI assistant that new hires could "ask anything" without judgment—trained on company-specific knowledge and integrated with Slack/Teams.

**Outcome:** 6-month retention improved to 89%. New hire time-to-productivity reduced by 40%.

# ✅ Five Actionable Takeaways for E-Learning & AI

## Takeaway 1: Never Mention Your Solution First

**The Principle:** The moment you share your training idea, AI tool, or course concept, you've biased the conversation.

**The Action:**
- Start every needs analysis conversation with questions about problems
- Save your solution for the *end*—or better yet, another conversation entirely
- If someone asks "What are you thinking of building?" say "I want to understand the problem first before I bias you with my ideas"

**The AI Connection:** Before building AI features, interview users about how they currently solve problems. Don't ask "Would AI help?" Ask "Walk me through how you handle this now."

---

## Takeaway 2: Seek Specific Stories, Not General Opinions

**The Principle:** "I always complete my training" is fluff. "Last month I finished 2 out of 5 assigned courses because I had a project deadline" is gold.

**The Action:**
- Follow every generalization with "Can you give me a specific example?"
- When someone says "usually," ask "When was the last time?"
- Replace "Would you..." with "When did you last..."
- Document exact quotes—specifics matter more than summaries

**The AI Connection:** When researching AI feature adoption, ask about actual AI tool usage. "Have you used ChatGPT for work? Show me how you used it last week."

---

## Takeaway 3: Look for Evidence of Pain, Not Expressions of Interest

**The Principle:** People expressing interest in your idea costs them nothing. People who have spent time, money, or energy on the problem prove it matters.

**The Action:**
- Ask about workarounds they've created (proves problem exists)
- Ask about money they've spent on solutions (proves problem is costly)
- Ask about time they've wasted (proves problem matters)
- Look for complaints, not compliments

**The AI Connection:** If someone says they'd "love" an AI tutor, ask: "Have you ever paid for any learning resource? What did you buy?" Real commitment beats stated interest.

---

## Takeaway 4: Commitment Is Your Validation Metric

**The Principle:** A successful conversation ends with a commitment, not a compliment.

**The Action:**
- End every conversation with an ask
- Escalating commitment levels: Introduction < Time < Reputation < Money
- If someone won't give a small commitment, their "great idea!" feedback is worthless
- Track commitments, not compliments

**The AI Connection:** Before building an AI feature, ask: "Would you be willing to test an early prototype next week?" If no, the feature isn't validated.

---

## Takeaway 5: Keep Having Conversations Until You Can Predict the Answers

**The Principle:** You know you've done enough research when you can accurately predict what the next person will say.

**The Action:**
- Conduct conversations in batches of 5
- After each batch, assess: "Am I still surprised?"
- When patterns are clear and predictable, you're ready to build
- When every conversation feels different, you need more research

**The AI Connection:** Before training an AI model on user needs, verify you have consistent, predictable patterns from user research. Garbage in, garbage out.

# ❓ Five Reflection Questions

1. **When was the last time you started a needs analysis conversation by asking about problems instead of presenting solutions?** What would change if you committed to never mentioning your training idea until you'd heard at least 10 specific problem stories?

2. **What's the most expensive piece of "nice" feedback you've ever received?** Think of a time when someone told you your idea was great, but their actions (or lack thereof) proved otherwise. What would The Mom Test have helped you discover earlier?

3. **If you were building an AI-powered learning tool, how would you validate that users actually want AI features—beyond what they say?** What specific questions would you ask? What evidence would you look for?

4. **Think about your most recent learning project. Did you end any stakeholder or learner conversations with a request for commitment?** If not, what commitment could you have asked for that would have validated (or invalidated) your assumptions?

5. **Consider the learners you're currently designing for. What are three specific past-behavior questions you could ask that would reveal whether they'll actually engage with your solution?** Remember: focus on what they've done, not what they say they'll do.
