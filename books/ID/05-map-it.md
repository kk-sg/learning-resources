**Read Time:** 20+ minutes

## 📚 Book Overview & Why It Matters

"Map It" by Cathy Moore is a revolutionary guide that challenges the traditional "school model" of training design and introduces **action mapping** — a visual, performance-focused approach to creating training that actually works.

### The Problem Moore Solves

In the learning and development industry, a disturbing pattern has emerged:
- Organizations request "courses" without understanding the real problem
- Training becomes information dumps with knowledge checks
- Learners pass assessments but don't change behavior
- No measurable impact on business goals
- Wasted time, money, and resources

Moore calls this the **"course mill" mindset** — the automatic assumption that training equals a course, and a course equals content plus quiz. This model, derived from our academic experiences (kindergarten through college), treats employees like blank slates whose heads need to be filled with knowledge.

### The Core Premise

**Training is rarely the solution.** And when it is, it should focus on **what people need to DO**, not what they need to KNOW.

Action mapping is a streamlined mashup of:
- **Performance consulting** (identifying the real problem)
- **Backward design** (starting with the end goal)
- **Behavioral focus** (emphasizing actions over information)

### Why This Matters for E-Learning & AI

In the age of AI-assisted learning and digital transformation, action mapping becomes even more critical:

1. **AI Can Generate Content** — But content isn't the solution. Action mapping helps you identify whether training is even needed.
2. **Personalized Learning Paths** — Require clear behavioral goals to be effective
3. **Adaptive Learning Systems** — Need specific performance metrics to adapt around
4. **ROI Measurement** — Action mapping starts with business goals, making measurement natural
5. **Efficiency** — In a world of information overload, focusing only on what's necessary is crucial

## 🎯 Core Framework: The Action Mapping Process

Action mapping is a **four-step visual process** that keeps training focused on performance:

### The Four Steps

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    ACTION MAPPING PROCESS                    │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                               │
│  1. IDENTIFY THE BUSINESS GOAL                               │
│     ↓                                                         │
│     What organizational metric needs to change?              │
│                                                               │
│  2. IDENTIFY WHAT PEOPLE NEED TO DO                          │
│     ↓                                                         │
│     What specific behaviors will achieve the goal?           │
│                                                               │
│  3. DESIGN PRACTICE ACTIVITIES                               │
│     ↓                                                         │
│     How can people practice these behaviors realistically?   │
│                                                              
│  4. IDENTIFY NECESSARY INFORMATION ONLY                      │
│     ↓                                                         │
│     What's the minimum info needed for each activity?        │
│                                                               │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Step 1: Identify the Business Goal

**Purpose:** Commit to measurably improving business performance

**Key Questions:**
- What are you currently measuring that shows you have a problem?
- How would you like that measure to change?
- By when?
- By how much?

**Example:**
- ❌ "We need customer service training"
- ✅ "First-call resolution rate is 62%. We want it to be 75% by Q4"

**Critical Rule:** If you can't measure it, you can't improve it. And if it's not tied to business success, why are you doing it?

### Step 2: Identify What People Need to DO

**Purpose:** Define the specific on-the-job behaviors that will achieve the goal

**Key Questions:**
- What do people need to DO differently to achieve this goal?
- What are they doing now that's preventing success?
- What should they start doing? Stop doing? Do differently?

**Example:**
For the customer service goal, agents might need to:
- Ask clarifying questions before providing solutions
- Use the knowledge base effectively during calls
- Escalate appropriately when needed
- Follow a specific troubleshooting protocol

**Critical Rule:** Focus on BEHAVIORS, not knowledge. "Understand customer service" isn't a behavior. "Ask three clarifying questions before offering a solution" is.

### Step 3: Design Practice Activities

**Purpose:** Create realistic activities that help people practice what they need to do

**Key Questions:**
- What's the best way for people to practice this behavior?
- How can we make it realistic?
- What scenarios will they face on the job?
- What mistakes might they make?

**Activity Types:**
- **Mini-scenarios** — Single decision point (good for simple behaviors)
- **Branching scenarios** — Multiple decision points with consequences (good for complex decision-making)
- **Simulations** — Realistic practice environments
- **Role plays** — Live practice with feedback

**Critical Rule:** Activities should mirror the real world. Not "What's the definition of X?" but "What would you do in situation Y?"

### Step 4: Identify Necessary Information Only

**Purpose:** Determine the minimum information needed for each activity

**Key Questions:**
- What information is ESSENTIAL to complete this activity?
- What can be provided as a job aid instead?
- What can be linked as a reference?
- What can be omitted entirely?

**Critical Rule:** If information doesn't support an activity, CUT IT. Most "required content" isn't actually required.

## 📖 Chapter-by-Chapter Insights

### Chapters 1-2: The Mindset Shift

**The School Model Problem**

Moore argues that we've inherited a flawed model from education:
- Students learn to pass tests
- Success = knowledge acquisition
- Information is the primary product

**The Business Reality:**
- Employees need to perform
- Success = behavior change
- Action is the primary product

**Key Quote:**
> "As 'instructors,' we spend our days floating high above the real world in a cloud of knowledge. Information is all we have up there, so we've developed an unhealthy obsession with it."

**The Shift:**
```
FROM:                      TO:
"We need a course"    →    "What's the performance problem?"
"They need to know"   →    "They need to do"
"Content + quiz"      →    "Realistic practice"
"Knowledge check"     →    "Behavior change"
```

### Chapter 3: The Kickoff Meeting

Moore recommends a specific approach to initial client meetings:

**Don't:**
- Start with "What do you want the course to cover?"
- Accept "training" as the automatic solution
- Let SMEs dump content on you

**Do:**
- Ask "What's the business problem?"
- Request current metrics and desired metrics
- Identify the target audience and their current behavior
- Discuss potential barriers (environment, motivation, skills, knowledge)

**The Meeting Structure:**
1. **Set the stage** — "I want to understand the real problem, not assume training is the answer"
2. **Identify the goal** — "What will be different in the organization if this project succeeds?"
3. **Explore the audience** — "Who needs to change what they're doing?"
4. **Investigate barriers** — "Why aren't they already doing it?"
5. **Determine next steps** — "Let's gather more data before deciding on solutions"

### Chapter 4: Setting the Measurable Goal

**The Goal Formula:**
```
[Current measure] will [increase/decrease] from [X%] to [Y%] by [date] as measured by [metric]
```

**Example:**
"Customer satisfaction scores will increase from 72% to 85% by December 31 as measured by post-call surveys"

**Common Goal Pitfalls:**
- ❌ "Learners will understand..." (not measurable)
- ❌ "Employees will know..." (not business-focused)
- ❌ "We'll create engaging training..." (activity, not outcome)
- ✅ "Sales conversion rate will increase from 15% to 20% by Q2"

### Chapters 5-6: Identifying Behaviors & Barriers

**The Behavior Analysis Process:**

1. **List all behaviors** that could affect the goal
2. **Prioritize** by impact and frequency
3. **Categorize barriers** for each behavior:
   - Environment barriers (tools, time, support)
   - Motivation barriers (incentives, consequences)
   - Knowledge/skill barriers (what training CAN address)

**Critical Insight:** Only knowledge/skill barriers respond to training. If the problem is environment or motivation, training is a waste.

**Example Barrier Analysis:**

| Behavior | Barrier Type | Solution Type |
|----------|--------------|---------------|
| Agents don't use knowledge base | Knowledge | Training ✓ |
| Knowledge base is slow | Environment | Tech fix ✗ |
| Agents skip it to save time | Motivation | Incentives ✗ |

### Chapters 7-9: Designing Activities

**Two Types of Scenarios:**

1. **Mini-Scenarios** (One decision point)
   - Quick, focused practice
   - Good for simple, discrete behaviors
   - Example: "A customer says X. What do you do?"

2. **Branching Scenarios** (Multiple decision points)
   - Complex decision-making
   - Shows consequences of choices
   - Example: "Navigate a customer complaint from start to resolution"

**Scenario Design Criteria:**
- ✓ Realistic to the job
- ✓ Includes common mistakes
- ✓ Shows consequences (not just "Correct/Incorrect")
- ✓ Requires thinking, not guessing
- ✓ Matches the difficulty of real decisions

**The Scenario Question Checklist:**
- Does it present a difficult decision?
- Is it realistic to the job?
- Does it show consequences?
- Does it avoid obvious cues?
- Does it challenge common misconceptions?

### Chapters 10-12: Working with SMEs & Prototyping

**SME Engagement Strategy:**

**Don't ask:** "What information do learners need?"
**Do ask:** "What mistakes do people make in this situation?"

**Information Extraction Process:**
1. Have SME walk through real scenarios
2. Identify decision points
3. Ask what goes wrong at each point
4. Capture the "why" behind decisions
5. Document the minimum info needed

**Prototyping Approach:**
- Start with ONE scenario
- Build a quick prototype
- Test with stakeholders
- Iterate before scaling

### Chapters 13-15: Production & Delivery

**Batch Production:**
- Create all scenarios for one behavior before moving to the next
- Use templates for consistency
- Maintain a consistent difficulty level
- Review in batches

**Blended Solutions:**
Training is rarely the only solution. Combine:
- Job aids and checklists
- Performance support tools
- Manager coaching guides
- Environmental changes
- Recognition/incentive programs
- Practice activities (the training piece)

### Chapters 16-17: Evaluation

**Evaluation Framework:**

Since action mapping starts with business goals, evaluation is built-in:

1. **Did the business metric change?** (The ultimate measure)
2. **Are people doing the behaviors?** (Observation, data)
3. **Did they complete the activities?** (Completion rates)
4. **Was the intervention appropriate?** (Process evaluation)

**Key Insight:** If you did action mapping correctly, you already know what to measure. It was your starting point.

## 🛠️ Practical Framework: Action Mapping Step-by-Step

### Phase 1: Discovery (1-2 meetings)

**Meeting 1: Goal Identification**
```
Questions to Ask:
□ "What's the business problem you're trying to solve?"
□ "What are you currently measuring that shows there's a problem?"
□ "What would success look like? How would you know?"
□ "What's the timeline?"
□ "Who's the target audience?"
```

**Meeting 2: Behavior & Barrier Analysis**
```
Questions to Ask:
□ "What are these people currently doing?"
□ "What should they be doing instead?"
□ "Why aren't they doing it now?"
  - Is it a knowledge/skill gap?
  - Is it a motivation issue?
  - Is it an environmental barrier?
□ "What are the most common mistakes they make?"
```

### Phase 2: Design (Workshop with SMEs)

**Action Map Creation:**

```
BUSINESS GOAL
├── Behavior 1: [Specific action]
│   ├── Why aren't they doing it? [Barriers]
│   ├── Practice Activity: [Scenario]
│   └── Essential Info: [Minimum needed]
│
├── Behavior 2: [Specific action]
│   ├── Why aren't they doing it? [Barriers]
│   ├── Practice Activity: [Scenario]
│   └── Essential Info: [Minimum needed]
│
└── Behavior 3: [Specific action]
    ├── Why aren't they doing it? [Barriers]
    ├── Practice Activity: [Scenario]
    └── Essential Info: [Minimum needed]
```

### Phase 3: Development (Iterative)

**Prototype Process:**
1. Choose ONE high-impact behavior
2. Create ONE complete scenario
3. Include realistic context, decision, consequences
4. Test with stakeholders
5. Iterate until approved
6. Scale to remaining behaviors

### Phase 4: Delivery & Evaluation

**Delivery Checklist:**
□ Activities are accessible when needed
□ Job aids are available at point of use
□ Managers are equipped to coach
□ Non-training solutions are implemented
□ Baseline metrics are documented

**Evaluation Timeline:**
- **Immediately:** Activity completion rates
- **30 days:** Behavior observation
- **90 days:** Business metric check
- **6 months:** Full impact analysis

## 📊 Case Studies: Action Mapping in E-Learning & AI Applications

### Case Study 1: AI-Powered Customer Service Training Platform

**Context:** A SaaS company with 500 customer service agents wanted to improve first-call resolution rates. They had been using a traditional 4-hour e-learning course that covered product features.

**The Problem:**
- Course completion: 94%
- Knowledge check pass rate: 91%
- First-call resolution: 58% (industry average: 70%)

**Action Mapping Approach:**

**Step 1 - Business Goal:**
"Increase first-call resolution rate from 58% to 70% by end of Q3, as measured by ticket resolution data"

**Step 2 - Behaviors:**
1. Agents use diagnostic questioning sequence before offering solutions
2. Agents search knowledge base using specific keywords
3. Agents recognize when to escalate vs. solve

**Step 3 - Practice Activities:**
- Created branching scenarios with AI chatbot simulator
- Each scenario had realistic customer personas
- Decisions led to consequences (resolved, escalated, or frustrated customer)
- AI adapted difficulty based on agent performance

**Step 4 - Information:**
- Minimum info: Troubleshooting decision tree
- Just-in-time job aids: Keyword search guide
- Linked reference: Product documentation (searchable)

**AI Integration:**
- AI chatbot provided realistic practice conversations
- Natural language processing evaluated questioning techniques
- Adaptive learning paths based on individual gaps
- Real-time coaching suggestions

**Results After 6 Months:**
- First-call resolution: 71% (↑13%)
- Average handle time: -22% (more efficient)
- Customer satisfaction: +15%
- Course time: 90 minutes (vs. 4 hours)

**Key Takeaway:** Focusing on behaviors, not knowledge, with AI-powered practice scenarios delivered measurable business results.

### Case Study 2: AI-Enhanced Sales Training for SaaS Company

**Context:** A B2B SaaS company's sales team was struggling to close enterprise deals. Traditional product training wasn't working.

**The Problem:**
- Win rate on enterprise deals: 18%
- Average sales cycle: 127 days
- Reps knew product features but couldn't articulate value

**Action Mapping Approach:**

**Step 1 - Business Goal:**
"Increase enterprise deal win rate from 18% to 28% by Q4, reducing average sales cycle to 90 days"

**Step 2 - Behaviors:**
1. Reps discovery customer business outcomes before discussing product
2. Reps map product features to specific customer pain points
3. Reps handle C-suite objections with ROI-focused responses
4. Reps use appropriate proof points (case studies, data)

**Step 3 - Practice Activities:**
- AI-powered role-play with virtual CTO/CFO personas
- Branching scenarios for objection handling
- Real deal simulations using company's actual prospects (anonymized)
- Video recording analysis with AI feedback on talk ratio, clarity, persuasion

**Step 4 - Information:**
- Minimum info: Discovery question framework (1-page)
- Sales enablement library: Case studies searchable by industry/use case
- Objection handling guide: Top 20 objections with responses

**AI Integration:**
- AI conversation partner with different buyer personas
- Natural language analysis of discovery call quality
- Sentiment analysis to identify weak points in pitch
- Personalized practice recommendations based on deal history

**Results After 9 Months:**
- Enterprise win rate: 27% (↑9%)
- Average sales cycle: 94 days (-33 days)
- Average deal size: +18% (better discovery)
- Reps requested MORE practice time

**Key Takeaway:** Action mapping identified that the problem wasn't product knowledge — it was sales behavior. AI practice accelerated skill development.

### Case Study 3: Compliance Training Transformation with Adaptive Learning

**Context:** A financial services company required annual compliance training. The existing 3-hour course had 98% completion but zero behavioral impact.

**The Problem:**
- Training viewed as checkbox exercise
- Compliance incidents unchanged year-over-year
- Employees complained about "waste of time"
- No connection between training and real decisions

**Action Mapping Approach:**

**Step 1 - Business Goal:**
"Reduce compliance incidents by 40% and increase compliance reporting by 25% in 12 months"

**Step 2 - Behaviors:**
1. Employees recognize red flags in common scenarios
2. Employees know when and how to report concerns
3. Employees apply compliance principles in gray areas
4. Managers respond appropriately to reported concerns

**Step 3 - Practice Activities:**
- Scenario-based decision points (not "read this policy")
- Branching scenarios with realistic gray-area situations
- Role-specific scenarios (different for sales, operations, management)
- AI-adaptive difficulty based on performance

**Step 4 - Information:**
- Minimum info: Reporting process flowchart
- Policy summaries: 1-page per major policy
- Decision tree: "Should I report this?" guide
- Full policies: Linked for reference only

**AI Integration:**
- Adaptive learning engine personalized paths
- Pre-assessment identified knowledge gaps
- Struggling learners received additional practice
- Expert learners skipped known content
- AI detected "gaming" patterns (rushing, random clicking)

**Results After 12 Months:**
- Compliance incidents: -43%
- Compliance reports: +31% (more reporting, fewer incidents = healthier culture)
- Training time: 45 minutes average (vs. 3 hours)
- Employee satisfaction with training: 4.2/5 (vs. 2.1/5)

**Key Takeaway:** Compliance training doesn't need to be long. It needs to be behavior-focused with realistic practice.

### Case Study 4: Healthcare Training with AI Simulation

**Context:** A hospital network needed to train nurses on a new electronic health record (EHR) system. Previous system rollouts had low adoption and patient safety concerns.

**The Problem:**
- Previous EHR rollout: 34% adoption at 6 months
- Workarounds common (defeating purpose)
- Patient safety incidents from documentation errors
- Nurses frustrated, resistant to change

**Action Mapping Approach:**

**Step 1 - Business Goal:**
"Achieve 90% EHR adoption within 3 months with zero patient safety incidents related to documentation"

**Step 2 - Behaviors:**
1. Nurses document patient encounters in real-time (not end of shift)
2. Nurses use barcode scanning for medication administration
3. Nurses complete required fields without workarounds
4. Nurses use clinical decision support alerts appropriately

**Step 3 - Practice Activities:**
- EHR simulation environment with AI patient scenarios
- Realistic patient cases requiring documentation
- Timed scenarios (real-time pressure)
- Error consequences shown (patient safety impacts)
- Practice in safe environment before go-live

**Step 4 - Information:**
- Minimum info: Quick reference cards for top 10 workflows
- Video clips: 30-second "how to" for common tasks
- Superuser support: Identified experts on each unit

**AI Integration:**
- AI-generated patient scenarios (realistic variety)
- Natural language processing evaluated documentation quality
- Adaptive difficulty: Started simple, progressed to complex cases
- Just-in-time hints when users struggled
- Performance analytics for managers

**Results After 4 Months:**
- EHR adoption: 94% at 3 months
- Documentation errors: -78%
- Patient safety incidents: 0 (goal achieved)
- Nurse satisfaction with training: 4.4/5

**Key Takeaway:** Training on systems should focus on doing, not knowing. AI simulation made practice safe and realistic.

## ✅ 5 Actionable Takeaways for E-Learning & AI

### 1. Start with Business Goals, Not Content Requests

**The Shift:**
```
REQUEST: "We need customer service training"
YOUR RESPONSE: "What's the business problem? What metrics would change?"
```

**Why It Matters for AI:**
- AI can generate infinite content, but content isn't the solution
- Business goals provide the metrics AI systems should optimize for
- Measurable goals enable AI-driven personalization

**Action:**
- Create a "Goal First" template for all project requests
- Require business metrics before starting any project
- Track impact, not completion

### 2. Focus on Behaviors, Not Knowledge

**The Shift:**
```
FROM: "Learners will understand the sales process"
TO:   "Sales reps will use the discovery framework in 80% of calls"
```

**Why It Matters for AI:**
- AI excels at creating practice scenarios for behaviors
- Behavioral data is more useful for AI analytics
- Skills-based AI recommendations are more actionable

**Action:**
- Rewrite all learning objectives as behaviors
- Ask "What would you SEE them doing differently?"
- Design assessments that measure behavior, not recall

### 3. Practice Activities Before Information

**The Shift:**
```
TRADITIONAL: Content → Quiz → More Content → Final Quiz
ACTION MAPPING: Scenario → Struggle → Minimal Info → Try Again
```

**Why It Matters for AI:**
- AI can generate personalized practice at scale
- Struggle-based learning creates better memory encoding
- AI can provide just-in-time information when learners need it

**Action:**
- Start every module with a scenario, not content
- Let learners struggle before providing information
- Use AI to generate infinite practice variations

### 4. Use Scenarios, Not Information Dumps

**The Shift:**
```
INFORMATION DUMP: Here are 47 policy documents. Please read them.
SCENARIO: A client asks you to do X. What do you do?
```

**Why It Matters for AI:**
- AI can create realistic, varied scenarios
- Branching scenarios can be AI-generated based on real cases
- AI can adapt scenario difficulty to learner performance

**Action:**
- Convert every "tell" section to a "try" scenario
- Use mini-scenarios for simple decisions
- Use branching scenarios for complex decision-making
- Have AI generate scenario variations

### 5. Training Is Rarely the Only Solution

**The Shift:**
```
ASSUMPTION: "We need training."
REALITY: "We might need job aids, incentives, environment changes, AND some practice activities."
```

**Why It Matters for AI:**
- AI can support performance at the point of need (job aids)
- AI can provide ongoing coaching, not just one-time training
- AI can identify when training ISN'T the solution

**Action:**
- Always conduct barrier analysis (environment, motivation, knowledge)
- Design blended solutions, not just courses
- Include performance support tools
- Measure if the non-training barriers were addressed

## 🤔 5 Reflection Questions

1. **The "Course" Trap:** Think about a recent training request you received. Did you start by asking "What course do you need?" or "What's the business problem?" How might that conversation have been different with action mapping?

2. **Behavior vs. Knowledge:** Look at your last three learning objectives. Are they written as knowledge ("understand," "know," "learn") or behaviors ("apply," "demonstrate," "use")? How could you rewrite them?

3. **The Information Audit:** If you removed 50% of the content from your last project, would learners still be able to perform the target behaviors? What does that tell you about what's truly essential?

4. **Practice Before Information:** Could you start your next module with a scenario instead of content? What would that look like? What resistance might you face from stakeholders?

5. **Beyond Training:** Think about a performance problem you're trying to solve. Have you considered non-training solutions (job aids, environment changes, incentives)? How might a blended approach be more effective?

## 🎯 The Action Mapping Philosophy

> **"Don't create a course. Create a solution. And only make it a course if a course is actually the answer."**

The most powerful insight from "Map It" is also the simplest: **Training is rarely the solution.** And when it is, it should be the minimum viable intervention — focused on behaviors, driven by practice, measured by business impact.

In a world where AI can generate unlimited content, action mapping's emphasis on performance over information becomes even more critical. AI can help you create better practice activities, but only if you know what behaviors you're targeting.

**Start with the goal. Work backward. Cut everything that doesn't serve the goal.**

That's action mapping.
